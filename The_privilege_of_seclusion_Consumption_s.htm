THE PRIVILEGE OF SECLUSION:

CONSUMPTION STRATEGIES

IN THE CLOSED CITY OF SEVERODVINSK*

“The level of happiness was higher here than anywhere else.”1 2 Thus a
young inhabitant of Severodvinsk summarized the popular narrative about
living under the special condition of a closed city in the late Brezhnev pe-
riod. After this remark, he continued, referring to the privileged recreational
possibilities for the citizens of Severodvinsk on the Black Sea coast: “For
several weeks nearly every citizen of Severodvinsk could visit the holiday
resort of Severodvinsk’s submarine construction plant in Evpatoria in the
Crimea”.3 This remark indicates both a certain feeling of being privileged
and mobile, despite living in a remote closed city, and a nostalgic note of
loss in the post-Soviet period.

It is symptomatic that the speaker refers to the realm of consumption and
leisure as the main indicator of “happiness.”4 In the first place, this remark
has to be located within the changed context of post-Soviet leisure and con-
sumption patterns, but at the same time, it still bears the notion of a signifi-
cant shift in consumption strategies, which characterized the post-Stalinist
Soviet Union.5 Under Khrushchev, consumption became one of the central
arguments of ideological legitimacy. Alongside the enthusiastic promotion
of new appliances and the provision of Soviet citizens with private apart-
ments where the newly emerging consumerism could be celebrated, state
authorities sought to regulate modes of consumption and tastes, including
these issues to the realm of ideology. The new openness toward the West
under Khrushchev allowed for more information about lifestyles beyond
the border and even for some direct contact with foreigners. Despite all of
the desperate attempts made by ideological guardians to prevent or diminish
the influx ofWestem culture into the Soviet Union, the 1957 International
Youth Festival and the 1959 American National Exhibition in Moscow

became pivotal events, especially for urban youth, in their reassessment of
self-expression.

If during Khrushchev's 1950s and 1960s this phenomenon was confined
to quite limited urban subcultures of privileged young people who imitated
Western fashion, the stiliagi and shtatniki, it became a mass phenomenon
during the following decades, when the acquisition of fashionable clothing
went through established black-market structures. Under Brezhnev, the
public attitude and policing of potential ideological disloyalty attributed
to the consumption of Western products became less offensive and more
ambivalent.6 The Khrushchevian mechanisms of social control - comrade
courts and people's patrols - lost their enthusiastic impetus of earlier years
and became formalistic obligations. If stiliagi and shtatniki in homemade
flashy clothing had had to suffer official persecution and public reprehen-
sion, fashionably dressed youngsters in jeans and T-shirts in the 1970-1980s
were admired as well-to-do, modem, and attractive by a large segment of
Soviet citizens and, as a rule, left in peace by the authorities. The emer-
gence of a widespread semilegal black market for Western items and the
consumption practices attached to it attest to a changed mode of interac-
tion between the state authorities and Soviet citizens. A significant shift
occurred toward what can be called a flexible socialism that allowed for a
new quality of negotiation with the authorities in pursuing personal interests
and needs. This shift is often described in research on the post-Stalinist
and late Soviet eras as a change that tackled the preeminence of the public
or official spheres in the lives of ordinary citizens, freed them to a certain
degree from the social pressure of previous decades, and strengthened the
realm of privacy.7

To a large extent, this research focuses on consumption as a practice
that formed private spheres.8 At the present stage, the argument about the
changed relationship of the public, official, and private spheres oscillates
between two general positions: One follows the so-called binary description
of late Soviet society through oppositions of state and people, oppression and

resistance, truth and lie, and the formula of "double-think'’ as the dominant
behavior pattern of ordinary Soviet people as a pragmatic reaction to the
inherent contradictions of the Soviet system. Oleg Kharkhordin has argued
that, as in the post-Stalin Soviet Union, state control became both more
discrete and omnipresent, the realm of "privacy" could only be protected
by dissimulation.9 This model implies an antagonistic relationship between
two firm, clearly defined spheres, and the split in an individual who changes
his/her behavior according to the sphere and is also aware of contradictions
and antagonisms between the two realms. Boris Dubin has developed a
similar approach to the issue of public and private, arguing for a kind of
"schizophrenic" behavior of Homo Sovieticus in late Socialism, which was
guided by "double-think" and "double-speak.”10 Vladimir Shlapentokh, in his
analysis of the process of "privatization” in the post-Stalinist Soviet Union,
sees less antagonistic, but still clearly discernible spheres of behavior in
private and public.11" Ingrid Owald and Viktor Voronkov have modified the
binary model of private versus public into a triple model that includes official,
public-private, and private spheres, and allows for a certain porousness of
these spheres, but at the same time emphasizes the "social schizophrenia” of
Soviet citizens.12 These models are based on liberal concepts of a pragmatic
self that perceive the paradoxes of the Soviet system as such and seek ways
to accommodate to them.13

While still popular in current research,14 these notions - the binary
conceptualization of late Soviet society that consisted of dissimulating
citizens - have recently been questioned.15 The most radical challenge was
articulated by Sergei Oushakine and Alexei Yurchak.16 Applying discourse
and performance theory, they argued for the failure of binary models to
approach the Soviet experience due to the Western, liberal origin of these
concepts and the general Cold War context of the debate. Instead, Yurchak
focuses on a "performative shift” in the daily practices of Soviet people,
which originated in the disappearance of primary ideological meaning in
official discourses and rituals. It was the performative act itself, according
to Yurchak, that maintained social stability and enabled the simultaneous
creation of new meanings of official rituals. Yurchak offers, therefore, an
interpretation of late Soviet experience that transgresses binary oppositions
and sees a position "vnye'' (yne, literally, "outside'') - beyond enthusiastic
commitment and opposition as the dominant and characteristic pattern
of the epoch.17 Kevin F.M. Platt and Benjamin Nathans have challenged
Yurchak for his overly simplistic analysis of dissident culture and for ne-
glecting the subversive potential of what he sees as an apolitical position
"vne.” Platt and Nathans reintroduce to a certain extent binary opposition
and dissimulative behavior. Their argument focuses on political energy
that was concealed in the allegedly apolitical position of ordinary Soviet
citizens.18

Despite significant differences, these interpretations of late Soviet society
try to grasp what was called a "little deal,’’19 an "organized consensus,’’20 a
"transitional [era of] de-Stalinization and re-Stalinization,”21 a "stable modus
vivendi”22: the quality of social stability in late Soviet society under Brezhnev.
The problem with assessing these interpretations is twofold: They differ in
the notion of private sphere, and there is no consensus on the periodization
of post-Stalin Soviet history either.23 In general, the clear-cut opposition of
public and private draws on Western liberal thinking that claims universality,
but reproduces a theoretical condition rather than an empirically corroborated
historical experience and practice.24 To grasp the experience of Soviet people
within complex networks by an opposition of public and private becomes
even more difficult, as there is no exact discursive equivalent to these no-
tions.25 The Russian "obshchestvennoe” (public), "publichnoc" (social),
"chastnoc" (private), and "lichnoc" (personal) differ in their connotations
from their counterparts in West European languages. To move away from the
opposition of private vs. public, here I use the term "personal" to denote a
focal point of social networks, group solidarities of various kinds and sizes,
of interests, aspirations, desires, and tastes in which a person is embedded.

This article continues the discussion of the quality of social stability in
the late Soviet Union by examining people's behavior within the complex
net of social boundaries of the Brezhnevite society in the special case of
the closed city of Severodvinsk. The argument focuses in particular on the
negotiation of personal interests vis-a-vis social structures and the state au-
thorities by consumption practices. In what follows, I stress the situational
character of such negotiations. The argument is based on the assumption
that in everyday practice it was the situational setting that decided the limits
of the personal.26 This approach does not exclude a priori the antagonism
of personal and public/official and a possibility for dissimulative behavior,
but considers "double-speak” as just one possible pattern within the variety
of "flexi-speak.”27

The specific condition of a closed city with daily life dominated by the
production of strategic weapons assumes a stronger dominance of state
control and surveillance. One would also expect a higher level of loyalty
and commitment, a kind of "quintessence of Sovietness'’28 - due to material
privileges and the maintaining of ideological status. The question is what
kind of commitment was at stake here and how was it performed? The other
issue concerns the level or extent of negotiations between citizens and of-
ficialdom - was there less space for negotiation or more? What were the
intended and unintended consequences of these special conditions?

Focusing on three case studies that address modes of consumption in
late-Brezhnev Severodvinsk, I argue that in particular situations the city's
very structure and condition facilitated and produced new possibilities for
pursuing personal goals that transgressed ideological and spatial seclusion.
But at the same time, the realm of personal interests could only expand
through negotiation with the state structure, not in resistance to it.29

Severodvinsk within the spatial hierarchy of the Soviet Union

With the introduction of internal passports in 1932 and a rigid registra-
tion system for large cities, the Soviet leadership tried to gain control over
migration from rural areas and simultaneously contributed to the spatial
segregation of Soviet society. Since that time, one's residence laigely decided
one's place in the social structure. Major central cities offered the highest
living standards with the best available earning and consumption possibili-
ties. At the bottom of the spatial hierarchy was the Soviet village with its
almost complete lack of all such facilities. With the 1956 introduction of new
restrictions on the growth of large cities, the policy of territorial stratifica-
tion that implied the closing of large cities to internal migrants became one
of the central pillars of the Soviet social contract.30 A system of temporary
and permanent incentives had to manage the labor, living, and consumption
markets. Special cases of this policy were the closed cities and settlements
in a narrow sense, as sites of military objects and strategic weapon produc-
tion.31 These cities either emerged from scratch in remote areas (such as most
nuclear cities) or already existing cities were closed to unauthorized Soviet
and foreign visitors with the introduction of strategic weapons production
in local plants. The two categories of closed cities, established by Minatom
(the Ministry of Nuclear Energy) and Minoborony (the Ministry of Defense),
differed in their regulation of internal life and level of isolation. With the
beginning of the Cold War, the Soviet culture of secrecy received a new
boost.32 The closed cities disappeared from official maps and discourse, and
an extremely rigid control system was introduced, especially for the secret
nuclear cities. The cities of Minatom were surrounded by barbed-wire fences
and protected by soldiers. Migrants to these cities were subject to an intensive
screening process by State Security, and migration from these places was
also highly restricted.33 Another group of closed cities constituted centers of
scientific research, "academic cities” that were only closed to foreigners.34

Severodvinsk became the site of submarine construction in 1953 and
from 1955 on was the center of nuclear submarine construction. As a unit
within the structure of the Ministry of Defense, the city was subsequently

closed to foreigners, and with the resolution of the Council of Ministers of
July 27, 1972, the city received the status of a border area and was closed
to Soviet citizens who lacked special entry permission.35

The level of isolation here was less than in the nuclear cities of Minatom,
but greater than in semiclosed settlements such as the Siberian city of sci-
ence, Akademgorodok. Rather, there were several "forbidden zones." highly
protected by wire, with different levels of access for authorized people.36

The usual way to reach the city after 1972 was by flight to Arkhangelsk,
a seaport thirty kilometers from Severodvinsk, and by regional bus or car.
At the checkpoint five kilometers outside the city, military personal checked
all passengers' passports and their special permits. Unlike the secret nuclear
cities, Severodvinsk was not protected by a barbed-wire fence, but without
a permit nobody was officially allowed to enter the city. Without a permit
it was even impossible to by a bus ticket to Severodvinsk in Arkhangelsk.37
This special permission was also needed for direct train tickets. As a rule,
permits were given for work purposes or family reasons. For the population
of the surrounding area, Severodvinsk was a great shopping attraction due
to a significantly better supply of commodities and victuals. People from
neighboring settlements thus found ways to circumvent permit regulations
by getting private invitations from Severodvinsk residents.38 In fact, it was
possible to bypass the checkpoint, but only residents of Severodvinsk were
aware of this practice.39

In the mid-1950s, Severodvinsk became the center of nuclear submarine
production with the city-forming leading plant Sevmash, which assured
its residents a standard of living higher than that in the rest of the area.40

The legacy of the camps of Molotovsk (this was the name of the city until
1957) had already disappeared from the cityscape some years earlier4'1 and
gradually from the memory of its citizens too. The city's new image became
associated with a project of vibrant industrial modernity.

The population grew from 100,623 inhabitants in 1962 to 197,232 in
1979,41 42 and the influx was mainly composed of a freshly recruited labor
force: new engineering specialists and the military.43 The percentage of
young people between the ages of twenty and thirty-five was thus extremely
high. They were the ones most successfully addressed by the Soviet pro-
paganda of the cult of technique and romantic self-sacrificing work.44 The
romanticism and heroism of everyday life, extreme exposure to advanced
technology, and the conquest of new horizons and depths of the planet
were the key aspects of the Soviet patriotic myth, which assured people
working for the military-industrial complex, especially those in closed cit-
ies, a place of honor in Soviet society. But apart from enthusiastic appeals
to the romantic feelings of young people, material incentives for Soviet
citizens from all over the country significantly facilitated the recruitment
of workers for Severodvinsk. By the 1940s (beginning December 1, 1945),
the city-forming plant Sevmash (by that time plant no. 402) became the
bonus of the Far North. This allowed the administration to calculate higher
salaries using the "Far North coefficient.'’45 The Far North regulations were
renewed in 1960 and provided all citizens of Severodvinsk with significant
additional benefits: 10 percent of their salary after the first year working in
the city with an increase of 10 percent annually, an additional eighteen-day
holiday, and better retirement benefits (one year of work counted as 1.5
years).46 Another important privilege concerned housing: after two or three
years of work at Sevmash, specialists could expect to move into a modem
apartment with all facilities. Those five- and nine-story apartment blocks,
built during the late 1970s, even received a special name: "Severodvinsk
type” (Severodvinskaia seriia).47 At that time, moving into a modem apart-
ment in a central city such as Leningrad would usually take ten to fifteen
years.48 Realistic prospects of acquiring separate housing were one of the
main arguments for attracting young specialists, who were, in general,
privileged over workers.49

Material benefits structured Severodvinsk society into the privileged
"zavodskic" (plant worker) and the underprivileged "gorodskic" (city dwell-
ers) who were excluded from some significant privileges of Sevmash. The
average salary in the city was significantly lower than at the plant: In 1960,
the average salary in the city amounted to 102 rubles, while at Sevmash it
was 157 rubles. Toward the 1980s, however, the gap in salary was smaller:
a city worker earned 293 rubles and Sevmash employees—366 rubles. In
any case, the underprivileged city workers had higher salaries than workers
in the surrounding area and in the country in general: In 1960, 102 rubles in
Severodvinsk, 90 rubles in the Arkhangelsk region, and 83.1 rubles in the
USSR; in 1980, 293 rubles in Severodvinsk, 224 rubles in the Arkhangelsk
region, and 174 rubles in the USSR.50 51 The division between Sevmash work-
ers and the rest of the Severodvinsk population was manifest in the issue of
housing, too. The prospects of acquiring a modem comfortable apartment
relatively soon did not apply to employees outside Sevmash: the calculation
of the authorities was simple - the majority of workers outside the plant
originated from the surrounding area and it was unlikely that they would
leave the city because of the housing issue. It was obviously different with
the freshly recruited young specialists from other distant areas. The gap in
privileges between the "locals'’ and the professionals recruited from outside
was also apparent within the plant itself: the "locals" were the last to get
an apartment.511

During the 1960s, improved infrastructure with public transport, cinemas,
theaters, cultural facilities, and shopping possibilities replaced the camp-
like settlement with extremely severe living and working conditions in the
1930s and 1940s. While the general supply of victuals improved only in
the mid-1960s, laterthere were additional shortages.52 Nevertheless, even to
visitors from Leningrad, the city appeared to be better supplied. A scholar
from Leningrad State Forest-Technical Academy, who first introduced live
plants on nuclear submarines, tried bananas - a symbol of luxury and privi-
lege - in Severodvinsk in the late Brezhnev period.53

It is difficult to assess the exact level of material benefits of Severodvinsk
residents, but the general picture suggests that even if the imagined privileges
were greater than the actual ones, they were still enough to produce a certain
feeling of pride in the included and envy in the excluded.

Case I: Black market at the Sevmashvtuz:
Semilegal practices within the Military-industrial Complex

Sevmashvtuz, the abbreviation for the High Technical School at the
shipbuilding plant for submarines, the Northern Machine-building Enter-
prise, was a magical word for many Severodvinsk youngsters - a place for
social advancement, but not only in an official sense: material incentives
and infrastructural benefits enabled Sevmashvtuz students to make a profit
outside the realm of legal socialist earning.

Despite the rapid development of the city since the late 1950s, the edu-
cational infrastructure of Severodvinsk remained quite limited for many
years with only a few technical schools.54 It was only in 1965 that the first,
and up to now, the main institution of higher education opened: the Sev-
mashvtuz - High Technical School at the Sevmash plant, as an affiliated
institute of the Leningrad shipbuilding higher education school. The main
shipbuilding plant needed a well-educated technical elite raised within the
walls of the enterprise. Students could attend classes in full-time, part-time,
and "extramural'’ departments.55 Most of the students were registered with
the full-time department and studied in the so-called zavod-vtuz method.
This meant that they simultaneously studied and worked in the plant.56

Like other Sevmash workers who profited from social benefits and higher
salaries,57 the students also had special privileges in comparison with stu-
dents in other cities: Besides a salary they received substantial scholarships.
While the regular scholarship at universities and other institutions of higher
education amounted to 40 rubles per month, Sevmazhvtuz students received
more than 100 rubles. This was a significant difference that allowed them
to afford much more than their peers, and it is not surprising that other stu-
dents from Severodvinsk (who studied, for example, in Arkhangelsk) were
quite envious and perceived them as "well-to-do'’ people.58 Those from
Sevmashvtuz could even afford to fly to Leningrad for the weekend (the
ticket from Arkhangelsk to Leningrad was about 14 rubles in the early 1980s,
and with the student discount -7 rubles). In fact, some went to Leningrad
and from there further to Tallinn - not only for weekend entertainment but
also to profit from their privileged financial situation, namely, to trade on
the black market.

Despite all the controls of a closed city and the rigid security measures
at Sevmash, its own students could obviously participate in officially illegal
black-market activities. This is especially surprising as all students had a
certain level of access to secret documentation: the director of Sevmash
was simultaneously the rector of VTUZ. The topics of the students' theses
were directly connected to Sevmash's production, and subject to security
regulations. Therefore, students were allowed to work on their theses only
at school.59 Most likely, as in every other area, the black-market exchange
in Severodvinsk was semi-illegal.60 Sevmashvtuz students mixed with
traders at Gostinka, the space in front of and around the largest department
store on Nevsky Prospekt, Gostiny dvor, also the central meeting point for
Leningrad's special black-market profiteers fartsovshchiki). and acquired
highly demanded Western clothing to sell in Severodvinsk for additional
profit: the most popular items in the early 1980s were white, winter sport
shoes and down-filled jackets that were not sold in the regular shops.

The state structure, in particular, enabled Sevmashvtuz students to
earn additional money on the black market and to encounter ideologically
"suspect” Western goods: they had sufficient financial means and networks
through their school to Leningrad students. Their geographical mobility bears
a quality of informal social mobility too: in Severodvinsk they were admired
as elite by their peers who had no access to either channel: high scholar-
ships or connections to Leningrad. Their share in selling Western clothes
in Severodvinsk was not even challenged by the seaport of Archangelsk,
which was only thirty-five kilometers away and where seamen sold Western
clothes and other items on a quite large scale in a designated area of the flea
market that was known to everyone. But according to some contemporaries,
clothes on the black market of Archangelsk were not nearly as trendy as
those brought by students from Leningrad's Gostinka.6" The students of
Sevmashvtuz also traded in vinyl recordings, but the main supplier was a
plant worker, a foreman, who went to Leningrad on weekends and invited
potential buyers to his apartment. To be sure, this person, a passionate mu-
sic fan, was an exception among the fartsovshchiki. The VTUZ students
were not the only privileged group that could participate in such activities.
Musicians who earned good money at the local restaurants were also active
fartsovshchiki,61 62 It is difficult to estimate the percentage of Severodvinsk's
population that participated in these activities; some population segments
were not aware of them at all.63

In general, the phenomenon of the black market was nothing special for
the Brezhnevian Soviet society: it was a fully established sector of economic
and social life. The peculiarity here is the status of a closed city, which
enabled the citizens to participate in it but also precluded the establishment
of a particular space for it within the cityscape: Different from neighboring
Arkhangelsk or central cities, there was no special place in the city desig-
nated for these activities - they had to be secluded in the semiprivacy of
the living space. But at the same time, the activities were tolerated by the
security authorities, who were nevertheless well informed about the black-
market trade in the city through their professional and personal networks.

Case II: Negotiating the limits of private:
Direct contacts with Western tourists and KGB officials

The attraction of what Alexei Yurchak called the "Imaginary West"64
was as great for young people in Severodvinsk as everywhere else in the
late Brezhnev period. Given the status of the city as a closed settlement
with secret strategic production it was impossible for Severodvinsk youth
to meet foreigners in the city. Nevertheless, some could find their way to
making friends with foreign tourists - despite secrecy regulations - via
directly negotiating the limits of seclusion with the authorities, as the case
of one student from Severodvinsk demonstrates.65 66 While studying English
at the Pedagogical High School in Arkhangelsk, he continued to live in
Severodvinsk and commuted for his studies. His desire to meet a foreigner
personally arose not only from the general context of contemporary youth
culture, but in particular from his educational background:

How should we assess the kind of relationship this student formed with
the KGB officer? And what can this story teach us about negotiating personal
interests under the special conditions of a closed city?

The relationship between the KGB official and this ordinary resident of
Severodvinsk with no access to secret information appeared to be normal
and rather relaxed. Officials were obviously not seriously concerned about
the case, as the student had no access to plant and state secrets, but neverthe-
less wanted to have the situation under their control, in order to answer to
those above them and secure their own jobs. In this case, the officials and
the student who was so interested in Western goods and lifestyle could easily
find a modus vivendi, as neither side questioned the legitimacy of the other
and was inclined more toward cooperation than confrontation.

Relations between state officials and youth interested in Western culture
were not always as smooth as in this case. Pornographic films (or films
perceived by the authorities as such) from the West were clearly limited in
terms of what was permitted, but the outcome of prosecution was subject
to negotiation too. In the mid-1970s a person was sentenced for three years
and all of the equipment (the TV, recorder, and tapes) was confiscated. In
the early 1980s, a group of friends who were all involved in black-market
activities met in a private apartment to watch Tinto Brass's Caligula (1979),
a controversial film classified as pornographic even by some Western au-
thorities. After it became known to state security, they were all summoned
to the KGB office and threatened with sentencing, but left in peace after
the intervention of a senior official on behalf of one of the accused who
was his friend.7"

Although the case of the English-language student cannot be general-
ized, there are some striking parallels with the Caligula case, which enable
us to have a closer look at social networking and the entanglement of the
personal and the social/official. These cases do not allow a clear delinea-
tion of hermetic spheres of the personal vis-a-vis state authorities, but at
the same time, it is possible for a person to pursue and articulate personal
interest only in cooperation with the state by building personal relations
with its representatives. As to the issue of "double-speak,'’ in the case of 69
the English-language student, it is difficult to trace a kind of dissimulation
or the split of a person into a "true intimate self’ and an "official self’: He
does not exclude his role as the resident of a closed city from his personal
engagement with Western culture. But, at the same time, his cooperation
with state security does not mean that he is totally exposed to state authority
or is hiding his "true self’ behind a mask. What is particularly significant
is the open outcome of the negotiation and the commitment on both sides
to cooperation and networking.

The quality of the social networks of Severodvinsk illustrated in this
particular case seems to partially confirm the research suggesting that nuclear
closed cities are communities bound by fear and submission.70 Fear was the
student's initial motivation for contacting the authorities. On the other hand,
the student succeeded in pursuing his private interest in Western culture
and personal contact with a Western person. As both cases demonstrate, it
was the special mode of interaction with the authorities - the importance of
face-to-face everyday contact with KGB officials, who may live next door or
be close friends - that enabled the student to transgress to a certain degree
the limits of the permissible or allowed the others to escape prosecution.

Case III: Maintaining stability:
Discotheques, informal networks, summer holidays

A relatively stable modus vivendi between state officials and students
also embraced other areas of cultural consumption and leisure: student
parties at the VTUZ dormitories. Leisure activities were of course quite
limited in comparison to Leningrad or even Arkhangelsk, but nevertheless,
the prominent discotheques at the VTUZ dormitories could impress even
students from neighboring Arkhangelsk, and in two ways - again an openly
privileged material status and a somewhat "privileged” relationship with the
authorities. Every block of dormitories had a coffee bar on the ground floor
that became a dance floor for student discotheques in the evenings. Students
from Arkhangelsk attending one of these parties in the dormitories were es-
pecially surprised about two aspects: first, that Severodvinsk students could
freely consume alcohol sitting at the windows while the rest were dancing,
and, second, that almost all of those openly consuming alcohol held their own
bottles of vodka, but no police were seen preventing this or even present.71

These retrospective reminiscences must obviously be taken with caution.
The police were quite alert in Severodvinsk during that period and the local
party leaders were very enthusiastic about every initiative from the Central
Committee.72 Furthermore, the perceived high standards of living and con-
sumption possibilities as well as "good'’ relations with officials have to be
seen in relation to the status and image of the city as a trouble-free place.

To be sure, the supply of general victuals and even some special products
(wine, fish, smoked sausages) was in fact better than in the surrounding
peripheral area (so that there was an exchange of general victuals through
personal networks) and to a certain degree even better than in Leningrad,
due to both easier planning for a small city and priority status. But regarding
other consumer goods such as clothing or furniture, the situation was not
much better than everywhere else. And the inhabitants of Severodvinsk had to
find their way through the notorious blat (bribe) network. Some people used
the channel of restaurants on long-distance trains between Severodvinsk and
Moscow. Apparently it was possible to contact staff on these trains and to
arrange networking: some ordered deficit food (caviar, special sausages, bet-
ter wine), others much desired imported women's shoes or other clothing.73

So, even those inhabitants of Severodvinsk who were geographically less
mobile than, for example, the Sevmashvtuz students, could still find other
ways to acquire desired goods.

Social welfare at the Sevmash plant contributed much to both the stabil-
ity and mobility of its workers - as was the case with the aforementioned
vacations on the Black Sea coast. It enabled inhabitants of Severodvinsk not
only to use Sevmash's own facilities in the Crimea (in Yalta and Evpatoria)74
but also to spend some summer weeks at the seaside as "wild" tourists.75
Young people (such as students), in particular, were able to earn some
money as pioneer leaders at the summer camps of Sevmash in Evpatoria.
Even the citizens of Severodvinsk who did not work at Sevmash could
still gain access to privileged resorts through kinship and other kinds of
networks.76 The possibility of spending some time in the Crimea was gener-
ally highly valued in Soviet society and could be designated to an outsider
not only as a privilege and an object of pride but also as something that
maintained the coherence and stability of the community.

Conclusion

Regarding Soviet society in the post-Stalin era in general, Stephen Lovell
has stated it "was mobile, and it increasingly moved on its own terms rather
than those of the state.'’77 In concentrated form, this can also be observed
about the closed city of Severodvinsk. Encounters with Western goods and
people were possible even for the citizens of this closed city. But I would
add that while in Severodvinsk the society moved, it was the configuration
of special state structures and specific quality of local networks that enabled
the movement. The geographical and social mobility of Severodvinsk's
inhabitants was made possible through the state structure and welfare. The
ambivalence of privilege and subjection manifested in the status of a closed
city and personal networks in a secluded community enabled people to
negotiate their personal interests in particular situations. A special modus
vivendi between individual citizens of Severodvinsk or particular groups
such as students and state authorities rested largely on personal networks,
which contributed much to maintaining coherence and stability, attenuating
ideological and spatial restrictions, and absorbing conflicts.
