Industrial revolution and urbanisation in Europe in the late 18th - 19th centuries

To summarise: the early industrialisation affected only certain regions and branches of economy, had relatively low rates of growth, took place against the background of protoindustrialization.

Yet by the 1830s Europe had become divided in industrialised areas in the core and backward agrarian periphery-Scandinavia, the Iberian peninsula, Italy, the Balkans, eastern parts of the Habsburg empire, the Russian empire.

To what extent the development in the core slowed down industrialisation in the periphery is debatable. The industrial development in the West did depend on cheap raw materials and foodstuff produced in the backward East.

Certainly, within the Habsburg monarchy tensions between east and west were obvious: Hungary became the source of cheap foodstuff and raw materials. However market-oriented agriculture in Hungary got new markets in expanding urban centres in the Austrian part of the empire. The same observation is valid for Prussia and Pomerania.

Second stage of industrialisation - the 1840s-1870s - France, Germany, the US. Railways, machinery, metallurgy

Railways were especially important: created national markets, linked production sites with raw materials. The states supported their construction because of their military significance -enabled the states to relocate troops at amazing speed.

Railway construction stimulated the development of iron- and steel-manufacturing, mechanical engineering and machine construction. Railways became the major consumers in these branches of industry.

Their construction required unprecedented capital investments - led to the emergence of investment banks, which were the only institutions capable of providing long-term credits (at the earlier stage an entrepreneur could succeed with short-term credits, as he had to cover only the costs of raw materials and wages - something that could be done with working capital).

The first investment bank was founded in France in 1852 (authorised by Napoleon III personally) - Credit MobiIier, It provided the model for many provincial and Parisian investment banks - Credit Lyonnais 1863, Societe Generale 1864. These banks were generated money by selling out railway companies’ stocks.

In Germany first investment banks emerged after 1848. In contrast with

France, they became shareholders of railway companies, their representatives sat on their executive boards, and from the 1860s, when the need for capital investment in railway construction diminished, the German banks started buying shares in other branches of industry, thus promoting mergers and the formation of cartels in coal mining and metallurgy (agreement on the volume of production, on hiring labour, purchasing raw materials, the distribution of orders and marketing finished goods).

Most recent research, however, suggests that banks perhaps played lesser role in developing relatively backward regions. Even in Germany banks feared risks present in new branches of industry.

At this stage more sophisticated technologies were used - they required specialised technical education. In France engineers were trained in Ecole des Mines, Ecole Politechnique, in Germany-in Polytechnics and technical schools. Germany was especially known for its industrial laboratories that ensured the leading position of Germany in electrical and chemical industries, as well as car-manufacturing in the late 19th- early 20th centuries.

1873-1896 - the period of economic depression caused by the transportation revolution (railways and steamboats), as well as by the influx of agricultural products from the American Midwest, Canada, Russia, Hungary and Latin America. In the same period Germany and the US became serious competitors for British /industries, as Britain was lagging behind in technical education

and suffered from poor cooperation between financial and industrial sectors.

The third stage of industrialisation - from the late 1890s till 1914. Industrialisation reached Italy, Sweden, the Austrian part of Austro-Hungary, Russia, Spain and Hungary (to some extent).

New technologies - chemical, electric industries, car-manufacturing. Britain loosing its leading position to France, the US and Germany. Germany is particularly strong in newer industries.

By the early 20th century Germany had been producing 80% of all synthetic dyers, was the world biggest producer of chemicals; and by 1913 it controlled more than 1/2 of global trade in electric goods.

The countries that were latecomers to the process of industrialisation had a chance to develop these newer, hi-tech industries: Italy was developing car-manufacturing and chemicals, Japan focused of explosives and electrical equipment.

Gerschenkron (focusing on Russia) insisted that the countries that reached the take-off at this third stage of industrialisation had to pass it very quickly, otherwise they would have failed in competition with the world leaders - showed very high rates of growth (it could also be explained by the fact that they started from a very low level, which automatically gives massive increase in percentages).

Industrialisation is stimulated by the state, which needs to overcome backwardness in military technologies - therefore it focuses on railway construction and heavy industries. The transfer of technologies and foreign investments created an opportunity for a very rapid transition.

Indeed, Sweden and Russia had very high rates of growth (in Russia -8% increase in the volume of industrial goods in the 1890s; in Sweden -12% between it 888 and 1896).

By 1900 Russia had become the 4th world producer of iron, and took the Sth place in the production of steel. The length of railway lines in Russia increased by 87% between 1892 and 1903, the production of oil increased 3-fold between 1887 and 1898. In the early 20th century Russia became the biggest producer of oil in the world.

However, even in Russia textile manufacturing had been developing even before the 1860s, railway construction boomed in the 1870s.

Italy does not fit this model at all - its agrarian backward south certainly placed the country to European periphery, however in 1896-1914 it did not experience rapid industrial growth.

Recent scholarship suggests that the contrast between Britain and backward countries on the European periphery in terms of the state participation in industrial revolution had been certainly exaggerated.

The role of the state in the building of the British overseas empire should not be forgotten. We also need to take into account the structure of power and authority in each particular case. In Britain it was strong unified legislature with no regional parliaments and corporate privileges. State participation in industrialisation also could take the form of destroying feudal relationships in agriculture, guild control and limitations, as well as the state supporting infrastructure, creating legal instruments, such as limited responsibility and shares, development of technical education and mass primary education as the means of disciplining labour resources.

Class as a new category of analysis. Wealth instead of privileges.

The notion of 'class' was already used in the second half of the 18th century (Adam Smith) to describe distinctions within the estates. It was already visible, that the nature of wealth was changing, that men were defined by their incomes.

The french Revolution politicised the term ‘class’.

Abbe Sieyes ‘Qu'-est-ce-que le tiers etat?’: the third estate as the true nation, however in terms of real politics, he is addressing himself to bourgeoisie - wealthy, educated people.

The French Revolution is instrumental for forging the notion of ‘bourgeoisie' - with its particular social identity and shared political ideas. By the early 19th century the idea that society is fundamentally divided, and different classes are driven by antagonistic interests, becomes common place.

Bourgeoisie, Middle Class(es) - as those people

who stand between the nobility, on the one hand, and the working classes and peasantry, on the other. Relatively affluent, ‘clean’ occupation for men and relatively free from labour sheltered existence for women, children and old people.

The idea that bourgeoisie is becoming the dominant class is supported by evidence:

1830-31 two bankers, Lafitte and Perier became Prime Ministers in France,

Marx suggests that with the 1830 revolution financial aristocracy replaces

he nobility as the dominant class.

For liberals (Guizot): class distinctions are justified because they are produced by fair, rational competition.

The conservatives and socialists (the term appears with the revolution of 1830) are very critical. Yet before Marx the early socialists (Robert Owen, Pierre Joseph Proudhon) hope for a peaceful transition to a classless society.

Karl Marx (Communist Manifesto 1847; Capital 1867): capitalist exploitation and class consciousness as inevitable outcomes of economic development while the proletarian revolution would abolish class distinctions and exploitation.

In his lifetime, politics seemed to confirm his forecast: several waves of revolutions across Europe: 1830, 1848. 1871 in Paris.

Yet the tensions are loosened by legalization of trade-unions, by state-sponsored educational systems, compulsory military service, by various welfare schemes.

To what extent the 19th century was the bourgeois century?

Aristocracy retained its dominant position in politics, the military

The middle classes - fragmented.

Liberal professions - as a distinctive interest group. The bearers of liberal ideology, before 1848 they shared anti-governmental sentiments.

The civil servants - much more numerous group, esp. in the second half of the 19th century

To what extent the middle classes share the same (liberal) values?

Belief in human progress, national unity, education, constitutional government, family, individual freedom. Yet both constitutional government and individual freedom could mean very different things in the 19th century. Some of these ideas are shared by other groups. Middle classes supported different political parties and economic doctrines.

Perhaps, education, family, the position of women in a society, culture - as the lowest common denominator.

Culture and leisure - increasingly defined by middle classes. State and municipal patronage replace aristocratic patronage. The first public art gallery - Vienna 1781, the Louvre in Paris is open to public in 1793, the National Gallery in London 1823.

The middle classes become the major consumers of the new art. The lower classes are excluded by various means (letters of introduction, admission fees). Entrepreneurs start collecting art. Lit&Phil societies (Manchester 1781, Leeds 1819). Exhibitions and concerts, libraries and reading rooms.

Critique against popular culture, especially against music-halls.

Yet before the advent of railways, the middle classes mostly imitate aristocracy in terms of leisure pursuits (sea-side resorts and spas).

With the spread of railway network Brighton becomes accessible for people from London (2hrs by train): in 1862 on Easter holidays 132 000 eople go to Brighton - soon Queen 'Victoria moves from Brighton to the Isle of White. The British aristocracy migrates to Normandy and the Mediterranean. Cheap railway tickets - 1870s, when 3rd class train carriages appear. Thomas Cook’s tours - to the 1851 Exhibition in London.

Grands magasins: first appear in Paris in the 1860s (Bon Marche 1869: described by Emile Zola in Le Bonheur des Dames 1883). For the first time, one can buy clothes, footwear, underwear, furniture, jewellery, cosmetics and food in one place.

Target middle class women - a safe, respectable, clean and elegant space where a decent woman can go on her own. New technologies immediately used in these spaces - glass display indows, gas and electric light. Enforce rigid moral standards on their personnel - poorly paid yet provided with free meals, housing (in 1878 90 members of staff lived in Bon Marche): their leisure is highly supervised, as the staff should share the same taste with bourgeois consumers

In the late 18th century - organised in guilds, which regulate their economic activities, discourage competition, set prices. The guild is more than economic institution - it is a corporation that defines the whole life of its member, its social status. Nowadays, historians start criticising the idea that guilds prevented economic growth.

Marseille 1782:

4% of master craftsmen employ almost 20% of all journeymen - workshops with 9 or more people employed

80% of master craftsmen work in workshops with 3 and less people employed

Up to the 19th century many industries grew by multiplication of small artisanal workshops specialised in a particular branch, which were connected by a network of subcontracts. A master craftsmen retained the /core of permanently employed qualified labourers, and in better times / temporarily employed more hands with poor or no skills.


Precarious economic position, yet these people were very proud of being independent. Group identity built around their corporate membership. The importance of places where the corporation held their reunions - a church, a tavern. Shared values - collectivism, paternalism, hierarchy, discipline. By the late 19th century, with the spread of mass consumer culture, traditional artisanal culture vanishes.

In legal terms, the guilds were abolished in Spain in 1812, in Sweden in 1847, in Austria in 1859, in Germany in 1869-1871, in France in 1871.

The other factors, working in the same direction - population growth and /transportation revolution: most arts and crafts do not have enough work to / employ people, local consumer markets get integrated.

In Prussia between 1800 and 1843 traditional crafts employed about 15% of the population, yet the population increases by 50% - the actual number of people employed increases dramatically. In France, by 1830-1840, population increase and unemployment push people to the cities where they try to live by crafts - the guilds are overpopulated.

Statistics remains very problematic. ‘Workers' in the 19th century censuses were often employed in crafts and not as factory labour.

Quantitatively, the number of small independent producers is increasing during the industrial revolution.

Factory and small scale home-based artisanal production co-exist for a very long time, even in textiles.

In France, in 1906, half of the labour force was employed by enterprises with 5 or less people. In Germany, in 1901, 31% of industrial labour was employed by enterprises with 5 or less people.

Still, small-scale enterprises gradually lost their independence - work on subcontracts for larger ones.

Access to credit, raw materials, labour resources, markets

- increasingly depends on big business. Artisans get integrated into the networks of production and consumption controlled by big capitalists.

Early labour movement built upon this crisis: people who nevertheless persist working in this way or who live by retailing goods manufactured at factories.

The first half of the 19th century - as the period of mass protests: 1819 Peterloo massacre, Captain Swing riots in 1830-31, the Chartist movement, weavers of Lyon in 1831 and 1834, the revolution of 1830, the revolution of 1848. In all these cases, people defend traditional moral economy, they are against new technologies, high taxes and tariffs, demand ‘just’ grain prices.


In France, in 1830 artisans start publishing several newspapers targeting this social group in particular - L’Artisan, Le Journal des ouvriers, Le Peuple, Г Atelier. Speak against the loss of skills caused by industrialization, call for protection of small-scale enterprises from competition. In Germany in 1848 many deputies from this social stratum in the Frankfurt Parliament often call for protecting their corporate rights as/ourgers.

The governments brutally suppress mass protests: create class solidarity, in a sense they are much more efficient than socialist propaganda or economic conditions of production. By the 1860s artisans either become integrated into the labour movement, or share petty bourgeois values.

Petty bourgeoisie, low middle class(es) - small-scale entrepreneurs, shopkeepers, lower ranks of civil service, school teachers... No common identity, class consciousness. However, small-scale entrepreneurs and shopkeepers do share some common values -respectability as a mark of their (threatened) social status.

Yet they are vulnerable - depend on credit, raw materials, access to markets. More often they downshift and become a part of the working class. Their precarious existence define their political sympathies.

By the late 19th century in France and Germany tend to support right-wing parties. Their dream is that their children become white-collar employees (and this sector is rapidly growing in the second half of the 19th century).

Other factors that destroy traditional artisanal culture -repeated attempts by the middle class(es) to regulate and reform popular, plebeian culture: instill work discipline and temperance.

Traditional (artisanal) popular culture is transformed into mass culture by its increasing commodification built around leisure: music-halls, mass sport, circuses, mass circulation press.

Lower middle classes that aspire to become integrated into bourgeoisie seek different forms of entertainment: clubs, self-help associations, lectures, concerts, reading rooms.

Literacy and mass schooling produce a stream of publications -manuals on different branches of industrial productions: what was formerly a shared guild secret could be studied independently.

Working class(es)

Mechanisation and concentration of production process at factories change experience of labour force: separation of home and work, factory discipline, formalised rules of conduct, social distance between a worker and an entrepreneur is considerably greater than in a small artisanal workshop.

In those branches of industry where mechanisation considerably advanced (textiles), entrepreneurs face the problem of recruiting labour force: rely on women and children - as those categories who value their independence less.


At the early stages of industrial revolution, industrial labour is very volatile - people seek other employment. Even in later generations, industrial workers often see their condition as a temporary stage in their life.

The early industrial production still depends on season (once machines are powered by water, factories had to stop when water level in streams is too high or too low - lay people out). The steam engine and a growing production speed increase pressure on labour force.

The idea that industrialisation produces less skilled labour force - a contested idea. The very concept of ‘skills’, ‘qualification' is not neutral - it defines wages. In the 19th century qualification is increasingly associated with work performed by men. Female occupations are for ‘unskilled’ labour - precisely because women's wages are essential for economic survival of a family but they are complementary. Women take up jobs that men would never accept. Migrants, ethnic minorities are also associated with unskilled labour.

Working class women and their occupations:

Economic growth is achieved not only by mechanisation of production process but also standardisation of goods and division of labour: waged labour working at home or in small workshops - e.g. dress-making. Depend on the suppliers of raw materials and people who sell finished goods. Women employed in this industry learn their skills at home - sewing is not recognised as ‘skill’, seen as natural for women. Work at home, setting up a small workshop does not require big capital investments, depend on markets, very insecure in terms of employment.

Domestic servants - their numbers are increasing with the expansion of middle class(es). In the 19th century, servants become an urban social group. A middle class woman is not expected to do manual work. The largest sector of female employment. Working time is not regulated. Informal, personal relations between an employer and (her) servants, isolation from other people in the same position - a very vulnerable position. A growing spatial distance between servants and their masters - a separate entrance for servants to the house or apartment.


Social structures in 19th century Europe: the countryside

Even in 1900 vast majority of Europeans lived in the countryside.

Population of Europe ca. 1900 - over 400 million, with at least 84 million (50% of economically active population) were employed in agriculture, forestry and fishing.

Significant geographic variations:

in Bulgaria, Rumania, Russia more than 3A of economically active population on the eve of WWI were peasants,

in Austro-Hunggry, Portugal, Spain - over 60%, while in the Netherlands and Belgium - less than 1/3, in GB - less than 10%.

(Still, in 1870 the figures were 45% for England and Wales and about 70% for France, Germany, Austria and Sweden).

From the Enlightenment, the educated elites had ambivalent attitude towards peasants: admired nature as rational, useful, productive and picturesque world, yet feared conservatism, brutality and ignorance of peasants.

Three major processes that affected rural population in the 19th century:

•Liberation from personal bondage

•The struggle for political domination in the village after the dissolution of personal, feudal ties between peasants and landlords

•The spread of new forms of dominant culture, and the appropriation of traditional peasant culture by national movements

Emancipation of peasantry:

•personal freedom,

•equality before law,

•freedom of movement and occupation, abolition of orders.

Across Europe, the process takes about a century to be completed: from Savoy in 1771 till the emancipation of Roma people in the Danubian principalities in 1864. All together, 33 legislative acts in Europe.

The emancipation could take many years: 21 years in Savoy, 43 years in Prussia, 73 years in Denmark. In Russia the process started in 1762 (Peter III's manifesto on the liberty of the noble estate) till 1917 (the abolition of estates).

Even half a century after the abolition of serfdom in Denmark, landlords insisted upon their right to flog peasants on their estates. Russian courts sentenced peasants to corporal punishment even in the 20th century.

In many countries, landlords retained their seigniorial riqhts of hunting, fishinq, forest riqhts.

After the emancipation of peasantry, old landowning aristocracy begins to decline.

Some scholars (like Arno Mayer and Dominic Lieven) insist that ancient regime existed until WWI: the aristocracy and the church still dominated European politics in the 19th century.

Yet at the very same time other scholars (Geoff Ely and David Blackburn) demonstrated that the power of Prussian Junkers under Keiser Wilhelm had been exaggerated.

In any case, these studies warn us against oversimplified schemes (a transition from feudal class to bourgeoisie as the dominant class). Landed aristocracy retained considerable power in the 19th century thanks to its ties to big capital and commercialisation of their estates. Sharecropping helped old aristocracy and the new social stratum of rural notables retain their influence in Spain, France or Italy.

The driving force behind emancipation of peasantry:

•The spread of capitalist relations ?

•peasant uprisings ?

By themselves, do not necessarily lead to emancipation.

•Secular ideologies - the concept of human rights, the idea of a nation-state with all its mobilising potential - were important.

•However the primary impetus was the struggle between the state and the nobility. France was the only country in Europe where the abolition of feudal servitudes and personal dependency was initiated and carried out by the state that was not at the same time an absolute monarchy.

In Italy and Germany emancipation of peasantry happened mostly in the period of the Napoleonic wars and in 1848. The revolutions were not always beneficial to the process, sometimes they delayed emancipation, as they caused fears that the whole social order could be undermined. Napoleon's empire could also be seen as a synthesis of revolutionary initiatives from 1789-93 and the 181h century absolutist monarchy.

In Galicia and Croatia peasants were liberated by the emperor, in order to create a counterbalance to landed aristocracy in these regions - the Poles and Hungarians.

Military defeats of Prussia in 1806 and Russia in 1856, caused by technological and administrative backwardness, were also important triggers for emancipation process.

The emancipation of peasantry and modernisation of the state apparatus in the 19th century creates two political problems for noble landowners:

•How to respond to the new constellation of power in the countryside?

•How to behave towards the centralised modern state and its administration?

In the aftermath of the Napoleonic wars in Europe, the old aristocratic elites hope to restore their estate privileges and provincial autonomy, while Restoration monarchies prefer to rely on centralised administrative apparatus and police - the legacy of Napoleon’s empire. The conflict is typical for France, Spain, Italy, Habsburg monarchy, Southern Germany.

With the course of time, the methods of control change. In Russia in the 1880s the position of a land captain (земский начальник] is introduced to mediate between the state and the village: he represents landed nobility. In France, Spain, Italy a similar role is played by a prefect who links governmental administration and local notables. Even in Britain the aristocracy and gentry monopolised local administration. The old and the new elites learn to cooperate with the state in order to impose new forms of social control over the countryside.

Social protest in the countryside threatens landowning elites everywhere in Europe in 1848, in Italy in the 1860s and 1890s, in Ireland, Scotland, Wales in the 1870s-1880s.

However only in Russia in 1905-06 the nobility and the state almost lost control over the events. Old ties between the landed nobility and peasantry were rapidly destroyed by the forces of modernisation, while nothing apparently came to replace them.

The state, new forms of mass politics and new cultural forms (national culture) were penetrating village life.

Eugen Weber, Peasants into Frenchmen (1976): up to the 1880s, French peasants lived in a world of local, personal ties, had no national identity. It was only in the last two decades of the 19th century when they began to overcome their geographic isolation, archaic cultural practices, agricultural technologies oriented towards subsistence.

James Scott, Seeing Like a State: How Certain Schemes to Improve the Human Condition Have Failed (1998): describes the same processes in the categories of legibility' and ‘transparency' of the peasantry from the state perspective. Modern states are seeking to make population legible by standardising village life: introducing uniform weights and measures, single currency, abolishing internal customs and tax privileges, land cadastres, consolidating landholdings, assisting enclosures of commons.

Still, by tracing this process, we learn more about ‘ governmentality and the state than about peasants. In other words, unlike Weber, Scott claims that what is at stake is not peasants who become Frenchmen, but the French state that develops new technologies of governing population that enable the state to ‘read'/document peasant life.

A class with no class consciousness?

A famous statement by Karl Marx in The eighteenth brumaire of Louis Bonaparte’ (1852):

"The small-holding peasants form an enormous mass whose members live in similar conditions but without entering into manifold relations with each other. Their mode of production isolates them from one another instead of bringing them into mutual intercourse. The isolation is furthered by France's poor means of communication and the poverty of the peasants. Their field of production, the small holding, permits no division of labor in its cultivation, no application of science, and therefore no multifariousness of development, no diversity of talent, no wealth of social relationships. Each individual peasant family is almost self-sufficient, directly produces most of its consumer needs, and thus acquires its means of life more through an exchange with nature than in intercourse with society. A small holding, the peasant and his family; beside it another small holding, another peasant and another family. A few score of these constitute a village, and a few score villages constitute a department. Thus the great mass of the French nation is formed by the simple addition of homonymous magnitudes, much as potatoes in a sack form a sack of potatoes."

Marx claims that peasantry is inherently unable to form political parties, it needs to be represented, craves for populist regimes.

This idea was further developed by Eric Hobsbawm- peasants, as votes, are mobilised by other political parties, even if the fate of any political movement in the 19th, and even in the 20th century, depends on their support. Recently, this idea has been challenged by historians who emphasise active peasant participation in the 19* century politics.

Yet by the early 20th century peasant parties can be found only in Scandinavia and Eastern Europe: Bulgaria (1899-1901), the Czech lands (1899), Russia (SR-1901), Serbia (1903-1919), Croatia (1904), Hungary (1906-1909), Poland (1895).

All these parties defend traditional peasant way of life (and peasant commune) against the world of industrial capitalism. In Bohemia, Galicia, Poland, Bulgaria these parties struggle for extending the franchise. All these parties insist upon a land reform - from socialisation to creating small private landholdings - and promote agricultural cooperation. In Bulgaria and Croatia peasant parties consist mostly of peasants, while in Bohemia, Rumania and Poland attract other social groups, however their core is formed by educated peasants.

Rise of Population (1976) -explained the decline in mortality in the 19th century by better living standards and nutritional improvements and not by advances in medicine.

Except for smallpox vaccination (known from the 1720s, perfected by the late 18th century) mortality caused by epidemic diseases started declining BEFORE significant advances of medicine (Pasteur -1850s, Lister - sterilisation of medical instruments - 1860s, Koch -1870s)

improved nutrition affected the ability of a human body to resist diseases spread by airborne transmission, like ТВ, measles, influenza - their visible decline in the second half of the 19th century before mass vaccination

Recently historians are increasingly sceptical about McKeown's hypothesis: no proof that before 1850 nutrition was improving.

A roundabout way to measure the quality of nutrition - body size of soldiers, they do not always correlate with real wages and do not always demonstrate a tendency to increase.

Urbanisation probably caused lower nutrition standards.

Increased life expectancy among the elites in the 18th-19th century was not likely to be caused by improved nutrition.

Even erroneous medical theories could have prevented the spread of epidemic diseases, since they prompted sanitation reforms:

draining swamps, clearing of city slums, relocation of cemeteries outside of the city limits, sanitary legislation.

Reduction of deaths caused by typhus and cholera was important for general decline of mortality rates in the second half of the 19th century.

Water-pipes and sewage, as particularly important

Early water-pipes:

London - 1720s, sand filters installed in the late 1820s

Liverpool - late 1780s

Paris - 1780s (however an integrated system only after 1848) Moscow - 1805

St. Petersburg - 1859

Infant mortality - a key factor determining morality in general and affecting birth rates as well.

It was infant and childhood mortality that declined most conspicuously in the 19th century. Mortality rates among the adults started declining only after 1850.

Causes of infant mortality - mostly factors external to human body -infectious diseases, accidents, poisoning... The rise of the ‘child-centred’ family, better maternal care - instrumental for declining infant and childhood mortality

Visible decline in England and Wales, France, Sweden.

However in Austria, Germany, Russia infant mortality remained high even in the late 19th century.

Before 1850, infant mortality in Russia was above 300 deaths/1000 a year, by the late 19th century -260 deaths/1000 a year. Even in 1897 only 57% of new-born babies survived till the age of 5.

Declining fertility:

The concept of the demographic transition suggests that birth rates remained stable between 1500 and 1900 - in other words, it was approximating the biological limit and the population did not attempt to control them before 1880.

However it was not exactly the case:

North-Western Europe in the early modem period birth rates stood at 30-40 birth/1000 people a year (biological limit-40-50 births/1000) = below the biological limit. Birth control by late marriages and by a substantial proportion of women who never married (10-25% in the early 19th century)

In the 18th century a married woman of 20-44 y/o had

9 children in France

8.6 children in Germany

  • 8.5 children in Italy

7J children in Scandinavia

  • 7.6 children in England

Marriage rates - fairly stable over the period, as two principal factors affecting marriage rates (the age of the first marriage; the share of people who never marry) remain stable or slightly increase in Western and Central Europe. Even in Western Europe, before mid-19th century, fertility remained relatively high (although below the biological limit - birth control mostly through delayed marriages).

Two countries with unusual demographic behaviour:

France - low birth rates already by the late 18th century, with fairly standard proportion of people who never marry and an average age of first marriage -> some birth control method are already practiced!

Ireland - very low rates of population increase in the 19th century (4.4/1000 per year in 1800-1850, no increase in 1850-1900). Birth rates in marriage are very high but a substantial share of the population never marries at all plus late marriages

With exception of France, fertility rates start declining only from the 1870s-1880s. In Southern Italy or Spain fertility starts declining even later - from the 1930s.
