The population of Europe in the 20th century

Data: in the 20th century, governments understand well the importance of demography for social planning and increasingly rely on demographic research. Demography becomes a research domain on its own. A growing distance between demography and history, sociology.

Population growth in Europe:

203 mln in 1800, 408 mln in 1900, 675 mln in 1999

However, population growth was even more impressive on other continents: the share of Europe in world population decreased 1900 - about 19% of world population lived in Europe (25% if Russian empire is included)

1950 - about 14.9% (21% with the USSR)

2000 - about 8.4% (11% with Russia, Ukraine, Belarus)

WWII affected first and foremost the countries of Central, East-Central and Eastern Europe:


The growth of European population was achieved despite colossal human losses in the two World Wars:


WWI (death toll in Europe, lowest estimation, Russia included):

7.7 million soldiers lost;

5-9 million civilians (mostly died not as a result of a direct military action but because of malnutrition and epidemics - Spanish flu of 1918-19)


WWII: (death toll in Europe, the USSR is not included):

6,7 million soldiers, 10.8 civilians (mostly victims of genocide and ^air-raids)

The USSR: 12 million soldiers, 13,6 million civilian deaths


1950-2000: no demographic catastrophes of the same magnitude.

A steady population growth, particularly pronounced in the 1950s - early 1960s.

Later the pace of growth slowed down. In the late 20th century demographic problems (very low fertility) became apparent in Germany, Spain, Italy and many countries of Eastern, Central and South-Eastern Europe.

However, in Western Europe it was partially compensated by immigration, while in the countries of the Soviet block low fertility was also complemented by high mortality (Bulgaria, Hungary, Rumania) - the population started declining.

Fertility (birth rates):

• Fertility in Europe was declining. By the late 20th century in most countries of Europe, fertility dropped to below-replacement level.

Fluctuations: already in the interwar decades, fertility decreased below the net reproduction level in some Western European countries. A new sharp decline in the fertility rates in the West: the late 1960s-1970s. By 1975 fertility dropped below the level necessary for net reproduction in almost all Western European countries (in Sweden in 1968, in Denmark in 1969, in West Germany - 1970).

However, in certain periods and countries the declining tendency discontinued for a few years. Apart from shortterm fluctuations, there was a longer, one and a half decade period of rising fertility in the 1950s-mid-l 960s - ’the baby boom'.

The ‘baby-boom' held out much longer and was on a larger scale than would be possible if its sole reason had been the usual rise in fertility after the war. Moreover, it ended abruptly around 1964, followed by a marked decline in fertility, intensified in the early 1970s.

• With the course of time, differences between countries in Western and Southern Europe diminished, while in East-Central Europe fluctuations in fertility remained substantial, and that did not allow to discern any patterns in this region.

At the turn of the 19th-20,h centuries, Austria, Germany and Ireland had the highest birth rates, and family size was the smallest in France.

By the late 1980s, it was not only Ireland but also Norway, the UK and France that had the highest fertility, while the lowest fertility was observed in West Germany and Austria. Thus, France moved from the group with very low fertility to the group with high fertility rates, while Italy and Spain - the countries with high fertility for the most part of the 20th century, shifted to the group with the lowest fertility in the early 21st century.

In East Central Europe and the Balkans in the early 20th century, fertility was very high by European standards. However after WWII these countries showed remarkably different paths of development.

Unlike in most countries of WE and North America, there was no baby-boom in the region in the mid-1950s-mid-l 960s - instead there were substantial oscillations in fertility rates, which could be linked to constantly changing social policies. (Criminalization of abortion in Hungary in 1953 - did not last long, in Rumania in the mid-1960s - yet the population soon started relying on other methods of birth-control).

Hungary had average fertility rates (average for Western Europe), while Poland till the late 1980s had very high birth rates (only Ireland and South-Eastern Europe had even higher figures).

In the 1990s, the region again showed some similarity: fertility rates were dropping down fast in all countries of the former Soviet block, with exception of Albania.

The causes of changing fertility rates -a number of demographic, socio-economic and cultural factors. Similar trends can be caused by very different factors.

  • • The decline of infant and child mortality;

  • • Urbanisation

Indirect effect of these factors

Exceptions: in Italy in the late 20th century female employment was the lowest in Europe, at the same time fertility rates were also very low.

Economic theories of fertility maintain that the family size is conditioned by a household’s drive to maximise satisfaction by balancing the benefits against the costs of children.

In the 20th century the costs of child-rearing increased and the economic benefits declined for a number of reasons:

the decline of agriculture and self-employment, legal restrictions on child labour, compulsory education,

rising housing and educational standards and costs, growing female employment -rising costs of the time of women.

Cultural factors - individualisation and secularisation.

Philippe Aries: parents from the 1960s on are less and less willing to organize their lives around their children and try to arrange their affairs so that it would happen the other way round, i.e, they organize child-rearing around their lives.

The weakening of religious values, as well as the identification with other traditional institutions of society.

These structural explanations could be applied to East-Central Europe and the Balkans in the 20th century, even declining fertility in the region in the mid-1950s-1960s. It was the period when the region experienced a significant reduction of population employed in agriculture - the group that was primarily responsible for very high birth rates in earlier periods.

Mortality

•Significantly decreased everywhere, however with regionally diverging dynamics.

In the early 20th century the lowest mortality rates were observed in Scandinavia (even there an average life expectancy for men at birth was just about 50 y/o).

By the late 1980s in all countries of WE it exceeded 70 y/o and in Norway and Sweden approached 75.

Among women life expectancy increased even more: by 1990 in WE average life expectancy for women was 6.5 years more than for men.

The longer life span of women is a modern phenomenon, which could be observed in certain parts of Europe (Switzerland, Sweden) already at the end of the eighteenth century. In the early twentieth century, the gap was about 2- 3 years generally, but then it began to widen. By 1990, even the narrowest gap amounted to 5.5 years (Ireland).

Nevertheless, from the 1980s on, the growth of the gap between male and female mortality slowed down, and in a few countries, a moderate process of levelling off began.

Infant mortality (babies younger than 1 y/o) - the most important contributor to mortality improvement. Improved all through the 20th century:

in 1900 in Germany infant mortality was about 20%, in Sweden and Norway - just a bit lower than 10%. By the late 1980s in WE it was 10s 0.9% (9 babies per 1000).

The decline of infant mortality was particularly visible between 1950 and 1990, when it decline 4 times.

East Central Europe and especially the Balkans were lagging behind through the whole 20th century.

The decline of mortality in the region was particularly significant in the 1950s- the first half of the 1960s. Internal mortality differences in the countries of the region moderated and Czechoslovakia and Hungary caught up with several Western European countries.

However from the mid-1960s the figures for mortality in the region began to stagnate or deteriorate. This was the commencement of strong divergence in mortality between Eastern and Western European trends, which was further strengthened by the rapid unification within Western Europe in the 1980s and the post-communist ’transition period'

The change did not affect every country, age group, social class and gender in the same way. Women’s mortality rates turned relatively favourably, the mortality gap between the sexes was wider in East Central Europe than in most of the countries of Western Europe: 9 years when considering life expectancies in Poland in 1990.

Age and social differences also prevailed. In all communist countries, the deterioration of mortality affected middle-aged working-class males the most.

Causes of mortality changes:

The role of medical science and technology. Highly debatable issue.

Yet most authors suggest that even in the most advanced countries of Europe medicine starts playing a decisive role only in the second half of the 20th century. For most infectious diseases, mortality had begun to decline before medicine could have cured or prevented them through vaccination, which can be explained by belter living conditions, and most importantly by improving nutrition. Tuberculosis -largely eradicated in Western Europe in the first half of the twentieth century, even though its treatment became available only after World War II.

^Accessibility of information. The rising levels of education.

•A change in attitudes to infants and children.

•Better working conditions. Improved conditions of housing.

•Sanitation measures of municipal and central governments - building sewage systems, the provision of clean drinking water and control over the sale of food products -effects as early as the first decades of the twentieth century.

In the second half of the 20“ century health care became accessible practically to the whole society and thus medical innovations made possible by technical advancement benefited the masses.

The epidemiological transition:

a historic shift in the nature of fatal diseases.

Early modern period - the ‘age of pestilence and famine’, The 19th century - the ‘age of receding pandemics’, which remained predominant by the late 19th century. Infectious diseases as well as maladies of the digestive and the respiratory organs (e,g. pneumonia) were the leading causes of death.

The 20th century - the ‘age of degenerative and man-made diseases’ - cardiovascular and cancerous conditions and other types of degenerative health problems caused by human activity as the principal causes of death.

In the early 20th century these diseases cause 1 /5 of deaths in developed European countries, by the late 20th century-3/4.

Migration

European overseas emigration

The migration of Europeans to other continents: maximum between 1881 and 1910. 1.3 million people annually left Europe in the early 20* century. 27 million people left Europe between 1891 and 1920.

Emigration spread from west to east:

In the mid-19* century, emigrants were mostly from the British Isles,

0ter - from Germany and Scandinavia

by the turn of the 20th century - from Southern and East Central Europe.

Between 1890 and 1910, the main target of emigration - the United States (58% of European overseas emigrants went to the US).

Other important destinations in the late 19* - early 20* century - Canada, Argentina, Brazil, Australia, New Zealand and South Africa.

Many Europeans moved to the colonies as civil servants, soldiers or engineers (hoping to return to Europe upon retirement).

•The belligerent parties hindering both the emigration of their men of military age and the movement of citizens from enemy countries.

•The war made sea travel, and thus migration, difficult to manage and often even dangerous.

After WWI - the migration of Europeans did not return to pre-war levels.

Several reasons for that:

In 1921, the United States introduced restrictions on immigration (in order to reduce the total number of immigrants and to control their ethnic composition):

a 3% quota for each ethnic immigrant group (total number of immigrants from a particular ethnic group already resident in the UK as 100%).

In 1924 the quota restricted to 2%.

These measures aimed at the reduction of Asian immigration, but they also hindered Southern and Eastern European arrivals (because these regions had just started sending immigrants to the US, which meant a low quota set for them).

The Great Depression - made difficult to find a job in the US.

Thus, in the 1920s, on the average, 700 000 people per annum left Europe for overseas destinations, and this figure dropped to 130 000 in the 1930s. The US remained the most important destination for overseas migration.

After WWII: a new increase in European overseas migration. Half a million annually between 1946 and 1960.

However, the significance of overseas migration was gradually weakening:

•The strict restrictions on emigration in communist countries.

•A positive migration balance of Europe since the mid-1960s (more people arrive to Europe than leave it). The independence of former colonies induced a notable flow of repatriation (Frenchmen from Algeria, Britons from African and Asian colonies, Dutch from Indonesia and, in the 1970s, Portuguese from Africa). The persistence of strong economic, cultural and political ties with the farmer colonies, and humanitarian considerations - contributed to overseas immigration into Western European countries from the 1960s.

European immigration: flows induced by political factors:

Compared to the 19* century, the proportion of migration flows caused by wars and political factors greatly increased in the 20th century.

WWI and post-WWI peace treaties, which included the resettlement of ethnic minorities:

1 million Germans forced to leave Poland and Alsace

900000 Poles moved from Russia to Poland. About 1 million emigrated Russia during the civil war.

A flow of Armenian refugees from Turkey.

The Lausanne peace treaty: 1.35 million Greeks leave Turkey, 420 000 Turks leave Greece.

A similar population exchange between Greece and Bulgaria.

500 000 Hungarians leave territories annexed by the successor states of the AustroJHujiggrign empire and settle in Hungary.

In the 1930s, 300 000 fled Spain as a consequence of the civil war.

Population flows induced by WWII:

•Deportation and extermination of Jews: about 5.6-5.9 million.

•Workers coming over to the Third Reich. In 1944 - 7.8 million foreign workers, forced labourers and prisoners of war in Germany = almost one-third of the total work force.

From the Netherlands, Belgium or France - voluntarily, attracted by higher wages; from Eastern Europe most often recruited by force.

•In the last stage of the war, several million civilians fleeing East Prussia, Silesia, Pomerania and other Eastern German territories to the West.

At the end of WWII: about 40 million refugees registered in Europe.

Between 1945 and 1950 7.5 million Germans were systematically relocated from Poland, Czechoslovakia, Romania. Most of them ended up in West Germany. The 1950 West German census showed that 19% of the population consisted of refugees or emigrants. In 1960 - 24% (the East German emigrants).

A population exchange was also initiated at the Potsdam Conference between Czechoslovakia and Hungary; Yugoslavia and Hungary; and the Soviet Union and Romania.

3 million Poles relocated to Poland from the USSR.

Emigration from the countries of the Soviet bloc: 14.2 million people between 1950 and 1992/ 1993.

A large number of them - Germans:

The Potsdam treaty ensured freedom of movement between the Eastern and Western sectors of Berlin - until the building of the Berlin Wall in 1961 -3.58 million moved from East Germany to the West, whereas 0.5 million moved in the opposite direction).

2.8 million Germans left Eastern Europe - the largest numbers came from Poland, the Soviet Union and Romania.

In 1956- early 1957, about 194 000 emigrated from Hungary

In 1968-69 160 000 refugees from Czechoslovakia

In 1980-81 250 000 refugees from Poland.

In the 1990s: East Germans emigrating to Western regions of Germany; 4 million refugees with the disintegration of Yugoslavia.

European immigration: economically motivated flows

In the early 20,h century - few restrictions on the movement of labour within Europe.

950 000 foreign workers in Germany (mostly Poles and Italians). Many foreign workers in France. The highest ratio of foreign labourers in proportion to the country's population - in Switzerland (600,000 people, mostly Germans, Italians and Spaniards).

Between WWI and WWII: the movement of labour slowed down.

Bureaucratic rules made crossing borders more difficult than before World War I (minor obstacles compared with the restrictions after the Second World War). The Great Depression with enormous unemployment.

France was the only country to receive a significant flow within Europe (for some time, the number-one migration target in the world): almost 2 million foreign workers arriving to France between 1920 -1930.

The fast economic development after the Second World War. Migration within Europe.

In the 1950s - mostly Italians who went abroad to work

From the 1960s, Spaniards, Portuguese, Greeks, Yugoslavians and Turks joining the movement.

The peak in 1973, 7.5 million guest workers employed in the 9 countries of the European Economic Community (EEC) and the European Free Trade Association (EFTA) countries. The major host countries: Germany (2.5 million -12% of German labour force), France (2.3 million - 10% of French Iqbgyr force) and Switzerland (0.6 million).

The economic recession induced by the oil crisis (1972) - a plunge in the number of guest workers.

Changes in the countries of origin and internal composition of immigrants in the 1970s-1980s:

Instead of Italians, Spaniards, Greeks - Portuguese, Yugoslavians, Moroccans, Algerians, Turks.

A shift from temporary or returning to permanent migration (instead of primarily young and single men who intended to stay only for a few years to collect capital with which to start a family and a small business back in their home country- a chain migration = family members, relatives and even friends joining the guest workers, children born in the host country).

By the 1990s, another factor made the integration of the immigrants more difficult: migrants coming from an ever-growing number of countries and in ever-increasing numbers from regions outside Europe. Western European countries admitting refugees because of humanitarian considerations (from war zones, authoritarian regimes).

Between 1983 and 1992 Germany gave asylum to 1.4 million people alone. In 1994 in Germany, 28% of foreigners were Turkish and only 22% came from the European Union (EU).

In the early 1990s, 36% of immigrants in France were European (mostly Portuguese), whereas 39% came from the Maghreb countries.

In the early 1990s, 49% of immigrants living in the EU arrived from outside Europe.

As a result from the 1960s, the ratio of the foreign population had been rising significantly in every Western European country: by 1995 - 9.0% in Belgium, 8.8% in Germany, 3.4% in Great Britain, 4.7% in the Netherlands, 8.5% in Austria, 5.2% in Sweden and 18.9% in Switzerland.

Note that 1) data on the ratio of the foreign population include legal immigrants only (the number of illegal immigrants estimated around 2 million in Western Europe); 2) in certain countries, immigrants may obtain citizenship quickly, while in other countries, naturalization is a more complicated process. Eg..: the high ratio of the foreign population in Switzerland originates from the frequent lack of Swiss citizenship even in the third-generation descendants of the immigrants.

Eastern Europe: the guest worker movement on a very low scale, except tor Yugoslavia.

Allowing citizens to take Western European jobs would have presented an obstacle to the maintenance of state monopolies on foreign currency; the shortage of labour.. Obstacles to the reception of guest workers - the low level of wages and the lack of the convertibility of currencies.

The GDR, suffering from extreme shortage of labour because of large-scale emigration, employed guest workers from other socialist countries - on the basis of bilateral agreements, with a strict rotation scheme of workers (a few tens of thousands at a time) - from Poland and Hungary, the 1980s, mostly from Metnam, Cuba and Mozambique.

Families and households in the 20th century

Marriage patterns

The Hajnal model - remained valid at least until the middle of the 20th century:

around the turn of the 20th century, the average age at which people entered their first marriage all over Western Europe was circa 28 years for men and 26 for women = high when compared with the societies in East Central and South-Eastern Europe. This pattern persisted even in the interwar period.

However, in Western Europe between 1950 and 1970, the average age at first marriage decreased significantly: by 2- 3 years for men and by 34 years for women. Another signal of the renaissance of marriage and family (the sudden rise of birth rates = the ‘baby boom' was the other signal).

Then, from the mid-1970s, the age at first marriage rapidly increased again, and by the late 1980s, the Western European mean was at the same level as in the early 20th century.

Lifelong celibacy in Western Europe

= the ratio of those never married in the cohort of 45- 54-ye avoids: In 1900 8% - 28% for men and 10% - 24% for women (the lowest levels in Germany and the highest ones in Ireland).

In the 20th century the rate of lifelong singles declined (for men - in 1900-1950, for women - after 1950). By 1990 female celibacy dropped below male rates (men - 10.7%; women -7.1%).

Eastern, East Central and South-Eastern Europe:

Ca. 1900: marriage was entered at a younger age and was almost universal

Before WWII: Hajnal'.s Eastern European marriage pattern faded in East Central Europe, yet no marked change in the Balkans.

After World War II - the ratio of lifelong singles dropped down, the average age of first marriage lowered (like in the West, the baby-boom), yet little or no decline from the late 1960s (unusual). Decline in nuptialit'-’ (marriage-rates) - only from the mid-1970s.

Still, until the 1990s, comparatively high ratio of married people and the young age at marriage = marriage had a monopoly in partnerships.

Interpreting family formation patterns:

For the 20th century we need to explain two major phenomena:

  • 1) the youthful marriage pattern and the near universality of marriage in the 1950s and 1960s (the baby-boom period). The willingness to marry was greater in Western Europe in the two

f

decades after World War I! than was known at any time in history before;

  • 2) the successive rise from the mid-1960s in the age at marriage and the growing popularity of alternative forms of living arrangements in Western Europe (accompanied by declining fertility).

Factors affecting marriage strategies:

  • • value changes (individualization and secularization),

  • • the shifts in economic and employment structures (industrialization, urbanization, and the marked increase in female employment).

The decline in the age at marriage had already begun in the 1930s in several parts of Western Europe - linked to the emerging disequilibrium in the ratio of the sexes (caused by migration, and war losses). The effects of these factors gained strength after World War II.

After WWII: economic hardship and increased female employment as unfavourable to family formation.

Unprecedented economic growth of the 1950s-early 1960s - an optimistic 'reconstruction sentiment’, which improved the willingness to marry.

From the mid-1960s - the resuming decline in marriage rates - as linked to the change in women's role in family and society: legal emancipation and the diffusion of female employment and education.

An equalization of gender roles reduced the attractiveness of marriage from an economic standpoint: the division of labour within the family is one of the advantages that marriage has over living on one’s pwn. Plus a growing popularity of unmarried cohabitation.

Modern methods of birth control (the contraceptive pill and intrauterine devices): lower fertility; encouraged cohabitation outside of marriage and affected the timing of marriages - the lengthening of the phase of pre-mantal partnership and cohabitation

The Eastern half of Europe after World War II:

Marriage becoming even more universal there than before - the result of the interplay of several factors.

'Marriage as an institution of partnership was traditionally dominant in the region. The isolation of socialist countries from the West inhibited or delayed any economic and social diffusion processes. Considerable social and cultural uniformity.

•A relatively high degree of social security made it possible to plan life's important events, such as marriage. Social policy prioritized married couples in the distribution of several services, most of all housing.

Divorce and the pluralization of family forms:

Divorce law was varied across Europe in the early 20th century and diversity survived until the last third of the century.

Finland abandoned the fault principle as the only grounds for divorce as early as 1917.

In Italy - no legal possibility for the dissolution of marriages until 1970. Spain - divorce legalized only in 1982

Ireland - 1996 (after a referendum that approved the measure by 50.3%)

Wherever divorce was possible, the break-up of marriages multiplied manifold by the midcentury.

From the mid-1960s, the leading countries were Scandinavia and Great Britain

In the mid-1980s - GB and Denmark (about 50% of all marriages ended in divorce), while in most WE countries - 30%

The East Central Europe:

The early 20th c. - Czech territories and Hungary - divorce rates among the highest in Europe.

After World War II - divorce laws liberalized earlier than in most

WE countries - divorce rates reached a very high level.

Compare Poland with Southern European Catholic countries -divorce rates were much higher.

At the end of the 1980s, beside the Nordic countries and Great

Britain, the highest union disruption rates occurred in East Central European countries (East Germany, Czechoslovakia and Hungary).

A characteristically contradictory attitude to marriage in the East

Central European population: the traditional marriage pattern survived in the region to the 1990s, yet divorce figures continually increased and were constantly above the Western European average.

Another important trend, common to Europe as a whole in the 20th century, - the separation of households from each other.

Budapest 1910: only 42.6% of households existed by themselves alone in one dwelling; the rest - lodgers, night lodgers or persons actually renting a part of the dwelling,

Budapest 1930 - 60.8% of households occupied a dwelling alone.

Causes of changes in household structure

Contraction of the average number of children of families, as a result of falling fertility;

After WWII in the socialist countries - collectivization of agriculture and the disappearance of domestic servants

The spread of one-person households. Caused by:

  • • the improvement in mortality plus expanding social welfare services -> older people were less and less dependent on their relatives (women overrepresented);

  • • In the 1960s, the number of young people, and especially young women, living alone rose -> a special urban lifestyle. Stagnation (and even decline) in the 1970s- 1980s.

• After WWII and especially from the 1970s, booming divorce numbers -> the ratio of singleparent families rose.

This trend was far less important for East Central and Eastern Europe.

Relationship between partners and attitudes towards children:

1900-1950:

Families became more egalitarian, their rigid hierarchies (meaning, most of all, patriarchy) eased up. Child-rearing became the centre of family life.

Related to increased female employment, improved women’s wages and broader changes in the nature of women's jobs.

1900: Austria, Switzerland and the Scandinavian countries - 1/3 of employees were women; a in Germany, France, Belgium and Great Britain - around 30%.

Increase during WWI yet followed by decline after WWI.

Uneven developments post WWII. Scandinavian countries had not been on top of the list in the immediate post-WWII decades but became the leaders in the late 20th century. Through the second half of the 20th century Southern Europe and Ireland lagged behind other Western European countries.

East Central and Eastern Europe:

In the early 20th century and in the interwar period, female employment in the region fell in the middle range in a Western European comparison.

Female employment rose rapidlyeven in an international comparison in the 1950s and 1960s. As a result of this development in the 1960s, the socialist countries, and, most of all, Poland and East Germany, were in the lead in Europe, with the Scandinavian countries.

After the mid-1970s, the female employment gap closed somewhat between the countries of East Central and Western Europe, because levels had reached their peak by then in the communist countries, although the upward trend continued in all Western European societies.

The characteristics of female jobs also changed as gender differences in education gradually faded.

From the 1950s, women's education levels rocketed throughout Europe and became particularly high in East Central Europe.

In 1960, of the Western European countries, only Finland (46%), France (41%) and Sweden (33%) reached or surpassed the ratios found in Poland (35%), Czechoslovakia (34%i and Hungary (30%).

Consequently, in East Central Europe, the ratio of women in several professions requiring high qualifications, such as teachers, approached or reached that of men's starting from the 1960s.

The existence of the ‘glass ceiling': the level of real wages and salaries was lower in the sectors primarily employing female labour than elsewhere.

Income and wealth distribution:

The Kuznets curve, named after the Nobel laureate economist Simon Kuznets, shaped as an inverted U = differences in income first increased in the 19th century, and then, in the course of the 20th century, began to decline in industrial societies. (Simon Kuznets, 'Economic Growth and Income Inequality’, American Economic Review, vol. XLV (1995), no. 1,1-28).

WE, ca. 1900: the richest 10% of the population commanded 35-40% of the total income.

Scandinavia, 1950: the richest 10% commanded 27- 29% of the total income.

In 1950-2000 the process slowed down

West Germany, France, Italy and the Netherlands, 1970 - the top 10% commanded about 30% of incomes. (GB - a more moderate income concentration, whereas the lowest values - in Scandinavian countries and Austria).

However, tendencies of levelling off have halted or even reversed in several European countries, beginning with the 1970s and 1980s.

The strata with the lowest income:

Before 1929 their position changed for the worse = the middle strata may be regarded as the winners of the transformations.

The late 1950s till 1980s/1990s - the income position of the poorest was generally improving. (Important: in the period of rapid economic growth after WWII, even the stagnation of the relative income position implied a considerable improvement in the standard of living).

In socialist countries, a tendency towards a fast levelling of incomes occurred after World War II.

1962, Czechoslovakia - the upper 10% of the population accounted for only 14% of the total personal income = the most egalitarian income distribution in Europe.

However, from the 1970s in the East Central European region, income inequalities were on the rise again.

Distinct forms of inequalities emerged with regard to the access to consumer goods and services.

In the 1980s, Scandinavian countries (and especially Finland) had the most egalitarian distribution of income in Western and Southern Europe.

Belgium and Luxembourg, followed by the Netherlands and West Germany - relatively small income differences characterized.

Portugal, the United Kingdom, France and Italy - the medium group Spain, Switzerland and Ireland - highest inequalities.

Remarkably, at the end of the 1980s, it was not the communist countries any more that had the lowest income inequalities in Europe; it was the Scandinavian countries and notably Finland.

In the 1980s the decline in income differentials halted, and even turned to increase again in several countries in Western Europe and overseas = as 'the great U-turn’.

The most marked changes occurred in the UK.

After 1990, a slight reduction of inequalities occurred in Western Europe, however the erosion of income equality in the postcommunist countries was dramatic.

In the United States, starting from higher original levels, the decline in income inequalities stopped around 1970, earlier than in Western Europe, then from the 1970s and 1980s inequalities increased at a higher rate than in any European country. Only Ireland and Switzerland had income inequalities equalling that of the United States by 2000.

Changes in wealth distribution

Wealth is more unequally distributed in society than current income. Nevertheless, less systematic historical knowledge is accessible on wealth than on income.

Inequalities of wealth distribution became considerably more moderate in the course of the 20th century, albeit equalization advanced at slower pace.

The moderation of wealth concentration in the interwar period was slow, gaining pace during World War II and in the 1950s and early 1970s, then stagnating from the 1980s.

Another sign of the fall in wealth differentials - a growing ratio of citizens accrued some kind of savings.

In West Germany in 1962, 60.1% of the population had a savings account, in 1983 - 90.3%.

Why did income inequalities decline between 1900 and 1970?

The extension of franchise - and therefore the power of lower-income worker and employee groups strengthened.

In the interwar period, the lower-income social strata were given franchise basically in all European conn tries -> the decline of liberal parties and the rise of Social Democratic and Christian Democratic mass parties, finding their major constituencies among employees and other lower-income groups.

The unionization of the labour force - facilitated the wage dispute

The emergence of the welfare state and its expansion after the World War II.

The socialist alternative

An increase in the ratio of employees, accompanied by a decline in the number and proportion of those self-employed. Traditionally, self-employed have had bigger differences in wages.

A declining importance of agriculture (low wage sector). Plus industry generates lower profits (as compared with the 19th century) - its higher higher profitability becomes more moderate compared with agriculture and the service sector.

Demographic changes: population growth in Europe slows down, reserves of labour force become smaller, social groups with low education gradually join general pattern of declining fertility ->unqualified labour becomes a scarcer commodity, its price rises.

Wealth inequalities - moderated in the 20th century by the increasing state redistribution:

a rise in the progressiveness of income taxes; property taxes introduced;

nationalization after WWII in East Central Europe or in Great Britain

General rise in income levels -> groups that earlier merely covered their daily living expenditures were now able to save up, buy property and consumer durables, such as apartments, valuable home furnishings and cars; the diffusion of savings accounts.

Elasticity of demand -

The demand for agricultural goods is physically limited (people can consume only a limited amount of food - unless, of course, the population is expanding). The demand for the goods of the industrial sector and especially for services is essentially insatiable.

The growth in labour productivity in agriculture and industry is faster - extra labour supply flows to the services

WWI — destroyed the Central European monarchies and discredited the political role of aristocracy. In Austria noble titles were abolished, in Germany castles, parks and other real estate expropriated in return for compensation.

Land reforms in East Central Europe and the Balkans in the 1920s-1930s — diminished the concentration of land (especially among ethnic minorities — Romania)

After WWII — aristocracy almost completely vanished in Germany, in East Central Europe — where the titles of nobility were prohibited from use. In other countries, like the UK, aristocracy survived, yet its position dramatically weakened. The aristocracy was able to preserve its power longest in Spain and Portugal — based on the concentration of landed property

The growth of 'functional elite' — leading positions in public administration, science and education, culture, the churches, the military, the judiciary. - This group considerably increased the diversity of the upper classes, the degree of their integration varied, strong subcultural differences, differed in the degree of their openness                The replacement of the elite in East Central Europe after WWII — not only the political elite but also the elimination of the economic elite with nationalization of big businesses and large agricultural estates

At the initial states in the formation of the new political elite, political considerations overruled all others. Later — a process of professionalization.

After the collapse of the socialist regimes in the region, the replacement of the former political elite did not take place on the similar scale as after WWII. Yet significant regional differences: the Czech Republic, the former East Germany, Albania — political elites were replaced.

The middle classes — prblems with defining this social stratum -in-between position; non-manual occupations

Identity taking shape in opposition to aristocracy and working class Middle class culture — rationality, emotional control, respect for knowledge and education, independence, family values

The old middle class of the early 19th century — owners of small businesses (craftsmen and tradesmen), practitioners of independent occupations (liberal professions)

From the late 19th century — the growth of salaried employees — their income, lifestyle, identity as middle class, although they did not own means of production — this stratum was rapidly growing in the 20th century— employees of the states but also employees of big businesses

Factors explaining their growth — the rise of welfare state, the growth of company size, the development of services

Their differentiation from manual workers and owners of small businesses but also from managers

Group identity even stronger among those employed by the state — often enjoyed special privileges with regard to remuneration, prospects for promotion and pension.

Internal divisions within this group — hence the categories 'upper middle', Tow middle'...

The transformation of middle class culture in the 20th century — some components diffused in a society as a whole, others faded.

Regional differences: in ECE and some Scandinavian countries — the middle class was traditionally small, the integration was less complete, the entreprenerial bourgeoisie remained weak, these positions were often filled with by industrialists or bankers of foreign/ethnic origin (German, Jewish, Armenian).

After WWII nationalization almost completely wiped out the old middle class. Only the white-collar (state) employees — their salaries close to those of blue-collar workers. A slow regeneration of small proprietors from the 1960s — the process most forward in Poland, Yugoslavia, Hungary — the distinct socialist version of petite bourgeoisie.

In the 1850s-1950s at least — a considerable cohesion based on a relatively homogeneous social position

A strong group identity, effective collective action through trade-unions The composition, the group consciousness and the social position transformed considerably, especially from the mid-20th century.

Its growth and then its decline in the 20th century:

UK, 1910 — 71% of all employees were blue-collar workers 1980 — 38%

France, 1910 — 49%

Causes: structural shifts in economy (from industrial to post-industrial society), structural transformations within industries (advanced technology requires less manual labour)

Internal differentiation of the working class in post-WWII decades: migration from rural areas, international migration of labour, women entering occupations that had been traditionally reserved for men

Rise in qualifications, improved educational opportunities for children (expansion of higher education in the 1960s), rising living standards — incomes and consumer habits started approximating the middle classes. Improved social protection -the achievements of the welfare state — the breakthrough happened in the 1960s. Mixed marriages — workers' wives found it easier to find simple, non-manual work in the service sector.

The waning intensity of class conflicts after WWII. Institutionalization of class conflicts — the influence of tradeunions and left-wing parties.
