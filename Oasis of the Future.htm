Oasis of the Future

The Nuclear City of Shevchenko/Aqtau, 1959-2019*

abstract: Focussing on Shevchenko/Aqtau, an urban laboratory of Soviet nuclear modernity
on the Caspian Sea in Western Kazakhstan, this study provides a close reading of Soviet techno -
politics in a long-term perspective. Encompassing a time span from the late 1950s to the present,
the article traces how Shevchenko evolved from a secret uranium mining camp for the Soviet
A-bomb project in the Western Kazakh desert into a testing ground for fast breeder and nuclear
desalination technologies and a showcase of “atomic-powered Communism” (Josephson), but
is now struggling with its transition into the post-Soviet era. Adopting a multi-dimensional
perspective to show the interconnectedness of political, techno-scientific, ecological and social
developments, the article highlights how the showcase city lent substance to the imaginary
of the Scientific-Technological Revolution both domestically and abroad but also tracks the
underlying decision-making processes in the Soviet nuclear establishment to reveal substantial
disputes over questions of military versus civilian use and safety versus technological prowess.
It analyses traits of the city’s social microcosm to reveal the strictly segregated, but closely in-
terconnected spheres of a privileged urban society and a sprawling, post-GULag forced labour
camp, and adopts an enviro-technical perspective to study how the Promethean project of na-
ture improvement eventually entailed grave environmental degradation. Finally, it sheds light
on transsystemic cooperation across the block divide and highlights how Soviet visions of the
future continue to shape visions of the future in Russia and Kazakhstan.

allegedly a perfect example of "how mans activity does not hurt nature, but on the con-

Gur’ev-20, as it was called initially started life in the late 1950s as a secret uranium
mining centre for the Soviet atomic bomb project on the desert peninsula of Mangysh-

which relied on cutting-edge nuclear technology to sustain a modernist model city and
a cluster of sunrise industries. Home to the world’s first industrial-scale breeder reac-
tor and the largest atomic-powered water desalination plant ever built, it moved into
the limelight of domestic and foreign media and became a hotspot of international nu-

Shevchenkos atom was as much a soldier as it was a worker, with both the city’s uranium
mine and its plutonium-breeding reactor earmarked to provide fission material for the
nuclear arms race.3 Similarly, expectations of a Communist future, as exemplified by the
model city, were offset by the remnants of a Stalinist past that remained all too present in
Shevchenko’s forced labour camp complex.4 Since 1991, the city - renamed Aqtau - has
been struggling with its nuclear heritage, but the technologies it pioneered continue to
inspire both Kazakhstan’s and Russia’s present-day nuclear strategies.

Through the prism of Shevchenko, this article seeks to open up a multifarious per-
spective on post-Stalinist nuclear history. Taking cues from Gabrielle Hecht’s agenda of
‘"tracing the social, cultural and political life of reactors”,5 it seeks to tightly integrate the
technological, environmental, social, cultural and international dimensions of nuclear
technopolitics - aspects that have hitherto mostly been studied in isolation from each
other.

The history of Shevchenko highlights a stretch of Soviet nuclear history that has yet
to attract much scholarly attention. Planned in the late 1950s and forcefully developed
in the 1960S-1970S, the city is representative of a second phase of Soviet nuclear techno-
politics that differed significantly from the initial era of “Stalin and the Bomb”,6 although
it remained linked to it by many continuities. Put succinctly, by 1953, the USSR had the
bomb, but Stalin was gone - a constellation that heralded far-reaching changes for the
Soviet nuclear project. In socio-economic terms, the dismantling of the GULag econ-
omy undermined the very foundations on which the nuclear project had been built.
In programmatic terms, Khrushchev’s advocacy of technocratic rather than charismatic
leadership at the 20th Party Congress - the Scientific-Technological Revolution rather than
the Cult of Personality - foregrounded the peaceful utilisation of atomic energy, and his
professed belief in peaceful system competition (further spurred by Eisenhower s Atoms
for Peace initiative) deemphasised the prospect of nuclear war. Far from rendering the
nuclear arms race obsolete, these changes nevertheless established civilian agendas as
a firm second priority in Soviet nuclear technopolitics. From 1953, it was the task of
the newly founded Ministry of Medium Machine Building (Sredmash) to transform
what had hitherto been a military atomic project into a comprehensive nuclear indus-
try with military-civilian double duties - a difficult venture that resulted in a peculiar
merger of post-war technocratic expert culture with a wartime legacy of all-out mobi-
lisation, improvisation and coercion. Thus, Shevchenkos reactor was designed by the
Institute of Physics and Power Engineering (FEI) at Obninsk, successor to a sharashka
where captive German scientists had worked for the Soviet atomic bomb project, and
built by OKBM Afrikantov in Gorkii, a company that emerged from what had been
the country’s largest artillery manufacturer during World War II. Overall planning for
Shevchenko and the nuclear industry in general was entrusted to a Leningrad-based
institute which had earned its initial merits organising the wartime evacuation of the So-
viet armament industries before it was co-opted into the nuclear project and developed
into a massive hub of nuclear-related expertise. And Sredmashs building industry grew
out of Glavpromstroi, which the ministry inherited from the MVD in 1955 with a labour
force of over 300,000 convicts and soldiers.7

The ensuing entanglement of civilian and military priorities and practices profound-
ly shaped the development of Shevchenko. The very discovery of uranium deposits in
Mangyshlak in 1954 and of oil and gas reserves in 1961 rendered the peninsula a point of
both military and civilian interest. Given the high investments required to develop the
remote desert region, a decision was taken to pool the resources of the atomic and the
oil and gas industries.8 It was a strategy that helped jump-start both uranium-mining
and oil production before the middle of the decade, but it necessitated a radical depar-
ture from the regime of secrecy that had hitherto been typical of the closed cities of the
atomic project. Inevitably, the industrial development of the region required a work-
force numbering in the tens of thousands, and as a result, planning for a city of 100,000
inhabitants began in i960.9 Two years later, the city-to-be was chosen as the site for the
Soviet Union’s first large-scale breeder reactor10 - a decision which was hard to justify as
a compelling solution to the energy needs of a region in which oil and gas were readily
available, but made sense if the aim was to isolate a novel high-risk technology in a re-
mote location where it would do limited harm in the event of failure. As for the reactor’s
intended usage, it was planned as a dual-use facility providing energy and freshwater (by
means of seawater desalination) for the city and, secretly weapon-grade plutonium for
the military.11

Beyond these utilitarian considerations, the symbolic significance of the atomic oasis
in the desert was not lost on the Soviet leadership. Developing the city into an exempla-
ry Sotsgorod became a pet idea of atomic minister Slavskii himself.12 In 1963, Shevchenko
miraculously appeared on the map of the USSR, named after the Ukrainian poet (and
compatriot of atomic minister Slavskii) who had been banned to a nearby Tsarist fort
a century earlier. Within a mere decade, it evolved from dugout mud huts to high-rise
apartment buildings and witnessed the influx of more than 70,000 people. (Another ten
years later, a new general plan already anticipated a city more than four times as big.13 14)
By the time the BN-350 reactor was connected to the grid and the nuclear water desali-
nation facility commenced operation in 1973, Mangyshlak had become a “landscape sat-
urated with power” in both physical and political terms.12 To conclude the development,
the region was ceremoniously granted oblast' status, with Shevchenko as its capital - a
highly symbolical gesture of normalisation in which Sredmash formally released the city
from its tutelage and handed it over to the regular state and party authorities.15 This, the
message read, was not to remain a secluded heterotope - it was the harbinger of a future
that was to become normalcy throughout the country.16

“Atomic-Powered Communism”: the Cultural Dimension

A recent volume has called the showcase projects of 20th-century scientific-techno-
logical prowess “dreamscapes of modernity”. Foregrounding the trajectory from idea
to realisation, editor Sheila Jasanoff argues that in order for ideas about scientific and
technological futures to gain assent beyond the bounded communities of scientists,
they “must latch onto tangible things” which “generate economic or social value”. It is
the outcome of this “coproduction of ideas, materiality, and sociality” that ultimately
decides whether a “sociotechnical imaginary” gains general acceptance or meets with
broad resistance. Obviously, given the high stakes technopolitical regimes have invested
in big science and technology, an affirmative view of these technologies becomes “inte-
grated into the discourses and practices of governance”, and challenging them is seen as
an act of subversion.17

Atomic-Powered Communism was undoubtedly one of the pre-eminent socio-
technical imaginaries of Soviet high modernity, and the nuclear oasis of Shevchenko
represented an ambitious attempt to invest it with concreteness. Sooner than expected,
it promised to bring to life the seemingly fantastic projects that Soviet nuclear physi-
cists believed to lie in the atoms powers, such as the transformation of the country’s
Southern deserts into lush gardens - a vision which Igor Kurchatov had presented to
the audience of the 20th Party Congress in 1956.18 That same year, a new pavilion dedi-
cated to Atomic Energy opened its doors at the Exhibition of the Achievements of the
Peoples Economy of the USSR (VDNKh), and in the VDNKh’s emblem, worker and
peasant were joined by an atomic scientist holding a nucleus high above his head to
herald the blessings that the peaceful atom was to bestow upon the national economy.19
Shevchenko’s reactor moved into the limelight a decade later, when the atomic energy
pavilion was renewed to celebrate the 50th anniversary of the October Revolution and
scale-models of the BN-350, its water desalination plant and the city under construction
took centre stage.20 Another copy of the display was dispatched to the Expo 1967 world
fair in Montreal, where, alongside a model of the nuclear icebreaker Lenin, it acquaint-
ed the international audience with Soviet man’s Promethean conquest of the inhospi-
table Northern and Southern latitudes of the USSR.21 Seizing the opportunity to firmly
inscribe nuclear energy into the Soviet narrative of progress, Andranik M. Petrosiants
(Slavskii’s first deputy) claimed that half a century into Soviet history, the atom had be-
come both a symbol and a driving force of Communist construction. The completion of
Shevchenko’s atomic energy combine would set another milestone along this path, pio-
neering with its novel technologies the truly unlimited availability of two of mankind’s
most critical resources - energy and freshwater.22

Propaganda fully came into its own once the nuclear oasis had become a tangible
reality. Dubbed the world’s “first city of the Scientific Technological Revolution”,
Shevchenko was contrasted with Magnitogorsk, the epitome of the Soviet Union’s
catch-up industrial revolution, in order to highlight how the country had advanced from
coal and steel to oil and uranium, from blue to white collar jobs and from deprivation
to affluence within just a few decades.23 24 If Magnitogorsk had set Russia on the path to
Socialism, then Shevchenko was a “launch pad from which we catapult ourselves into
the Communist future”.22

However, not everybody shared this enthusiasm. As Jasanoff points out, translating
sociotechnical imaginaries into tangible realities is by no means guaranteed to enhance
their legitimacy. Rather, the more high-flying a vision, the more prone to criticism its
materialisation becomes if it is found lacking,25 and the nuclear oasis was no exception to
this rule. To cite but one example, a 1984 travelogue by dissident Viktor Sokirko deliber-
ately juxtaposed Shevchenko’s “postcard appearance” of “a garden-city which unites sci-
ence and socialism” with the omnipresence of convicts working on the citys numerous
construction sites behind barbed wire. Communist construction was still very much
the work of “slaves” and slaveholders, he observed and concluded sarcastically: “Here
you go with your garden city with your utopia come true, with your progress!”26 If the
privileged inhabitants of Shevchenko harboured similar sentiments, they were well ad-
vised to keep them to themselves. Only towards the end of the Perestroika period did
the discontents of atomic-powered Communism raise their voices. At the oblast' Party
conference in summer 1989, many delegates passed a damning indictment of the social
injustice, the strained relations between nationalities, the dysfunctional economy and
the precarious ecological situation of the city.27 Since the collapse of the Soviet Union,
even key actors involved in the city’s development have retrospectively reflected on the
inherent defects of the local utopia, the main focus being on forced labour, only to be
followed by ecology.28 Studying sociotechnical imaginaries can thus sharpen our un-
derstanding of the “particular forms of sightedness and blindness” that characterise a
society’s understanding of technology at a given moment, and how these perceptions
change over time.29

Technocratic Socialism? The Logic of Technopolitical Decision Making

To this day, accounts of Soviet nuclear history have tended to repeat a narrative which
Soviet authorities were always eager to convey. It is the story of a single-minded group
of technological determinists systematically realising an ambiguous programme of de-
velopment in the face of overwhelming military challenges and promising civilian op-
portunities.30 However, a look behind the facade of Soviet nuclear technopolitics reveals
competing agendas and shifting priorities which were constantly renegotiated between
rival networks of scientists, technologists and politicians. Shevchenko’s BN-350 was the
focal point of many such controversies throughout the 1960s, 1970s and 1980s. With
its novel technology, it required the scientists to weigh technological audacity against
security considerations; being a dual-use technology, it invited repeated controversies
over military versus civilian utilisation, and as a pilot plant producing ambiguous opera-
tional experience, it prompted disputes over the future course of breeder development.
A telling episode in the reactor’s history affords insights into what was at stake and who
the actors were.

In 1984, Gleb Pomerantsev, the BN-350S senior reactor physicist, wrote a letter to
minister Slavskii in person in which he suggested massively accelerating the introduc-
tion of large-scale breeder reactors for power generation?2 Arguing that the operation
of the BN-350 had provided sufficient proof of the technology’s reliability he suggest-
ed skipping the construction of further prototype reactors, maintaining that a series of
ambitious experiments at Shevchenkos reactor could provide all the data necessary for
realising an aggressive development agenda - a strategy that would, without a doubt,
have kept not only the BN-350, but also Pomerantsev himself in the limelight.

At FEI in Obninsk, the reactor’s scientific lead institution, senior nuclear physicist
Lev A. Kochetkov was not amused by these ideas. He cautioned that along the path to
serial construction, it was necessary to “first and foremost solve the issues of reliability
and safety”. For this reason, he insisted on adhering to a more gradual strategy, arguing
that the French, with whom his institute maintained close contacts, also advocated a
cautious approach?3 From the point of view of his institute, he argued, there could be
no doubt that more efficient reactors, while feasible, still required a tremendous amount
of meticulous research and experimentation, including the simulation of incidents, and
that “vociferous declarations of intent concerning unrealistic aims” were of little help
along the way.

Another of Pomerantsev’s suggestions raised Kochetkov’s ire even more. Alluding
to the possibility that the United States might have plans for military breeder reactors,
Pomerantsev had proposed extracting weapon-grade plutonium from the BN-350 s core,
again seeking to increase the relevance of his reactor by all means. Kochetkov resolutely
rejected the idea, arguing that while the BN-350 was certainly capable of the task, there
was no military necessity for it at a time when the Soviet Union was not faced with a
shortage of plutonium and no intelligence pointed to the existence of American military
breeders. After all, he wrote, Slavskii himself had given orders to operate the reactor as
a prototype for civilian power reactors - a goal that would be compromised by fore-
grounding plutonium breeding.

larger controversies. The debates about civilian versus military usage scenarios in par-
ticular had accompanied the BN-350 s development since the first blueprints. Initially,
it seemed like a foregone conclusion that the reactor would be operated as a dual-use fa-
cility while being advertised as a purely civilian reactor. There can be little doubt that the
military potential of breeder reactors was duly pointed out to the political leadership via
the channels of the CPSU Central Committee’s Division of Defence Industry and the
Council of Ministers’ Military-Industrial Commission, and in i960 confirmation was re-
ceived for designing the BN-250 (later renamed BN-350) as a breeder of weapon-grade

The above leaves little doubt that Soviet nuclear physicists understood themselves
as key technopolitical players who were well aware of the broader implications of their
work beyond science and technology and actively sought to leverage political support
for their plans by invoking economic and military arguments.32 Clearly the experience
of educating the country’s top leadership about their designs made them realise the con-
siderable authority they commanded in their role as representatives of top-level science,
irrespective of their often young age.33 However, their technopolitical involvement also
acquainted them with the insight that the political leadership s support was by no means
unconditional. When technical problems forced a lengthy shut-down of the BN-350
shortly after start-up, the reactor s designers were cited before the Central Committee
of the CPSU and told that they had already had enough play time’ with their new toy,

of force could not change the fact that in view of the political stakes invested in atom-
ic-powered Communism, dependence was profoundly mutual. This explains not only
why the nuclear scientists enjoyed continued political support, but also why the polit-
ical leadership granted them interpretative flexibility over an extended period of time
and shied away from closing the exploratory process.35 In view of what was at stake, the
Party clearly found it wise not to put all its eggs in one basket, even if this meant multi-

Nuclear Landscape. From Improvement of Nature to Environmental
Degradation

In ecological terms, Shevchenko highlights the conflict between technocratic “improve-
ment of nature” and large-scale environmental degradation. For better or worse, the nu-
clear landscape was a site of massive and deliberate human intervention in nature - a
hybrid space where nature was purposefully technologised so as to embed the socio-
technical imaginary of atomic-powered Communism into Soviet reality.37 To appreciate
the larger implications of the nuclear oasis, it is important to note that envirotechnical sys-
tems (as Sara Pritchard calls them) are by no means “niche products” of modern politics.
Rather, utilising and ordering land- and urbanscapes in rational’ ways has been at the
very heart of high-modernist state formation.38 Bolshevik Russia in particular respond-
ed to the vast and seemingly backward space that it inherited from Tsarist Russia with a
vigorous programme of nature transformation by means of infrastructural construction,
from Lenins electrification programme (GOELRO) to Stalin’s Plan for the Transforma-
tion of Nature. But only the advent of the atomic age promised to remove whatever limi-
tations had hitherto hampered the large-scale creation of "new natures”.39 With the true
fire of Prometheus firmly in their hands, Soviet nuclear scientists saw themselves "at the
onset of a man-made world”, as one of them put it.40

Shevchenko seemed like an ideal place to substantiate these claims. On the parched
desert peninsula, the utopian vision of turning deserts into gardens acquired a rational
quality, as the humanisation of hostile nature indeed appeared a necessary prerequisite
for its purposeful exploitation.41 It has been pointed out that technologising nature is
often legitimated by naturalising technology,42 and Soviet rhetoric certainly reflects such
tendencies. With the start-up of the BN-350 reactor, "Mangyshlak’s atomic heart started
beating”, and from the desalination plant "a man-made river” poured into the desert and
made it bloom.43 Before long, the city boasted 60,000 trees, two million bushes, and
15 hectares of lawn and flower beds - enough for the self-proclaimed Promethei of Man-
gyshlak44 to decorate it with the epithet of a "garden city”45 and to claim that they had
not just "cured” Mangyshlak’s nature of "diseases”,46 but also performed "plastic surgery”
on its face.47 And the "nuclear oasis” was only the beginning, they insisted. There would
come a time when they transformed "all [of Mangyslak] into a flowering garden”48 and
when the peninsula’s improved nature would be able not only to quench the thirst of its
inhabitants - already numbering in the hundreds of thousands - but also to feed them.
In the near future, nuclear water desalination was expected to become cheap enough
for large-scale irrigation projects,49 and in line with these forecasts Jurii A. Koreisho, the
director of Shevchenko’s uranium combine PGMK, declared agriculture a top priority
in 1973. Proudly referring to the hothouses where his enterprise was already growing
cucumber and tomatoes on desalinated water, he solemnly proclaimed "that this effort
is necessary, and that it does not represent some kind of 'hobby’, as some comrades
apparently think”.50 Agronomists of Leningrad-based Lengiprovodkhoz concurred and
calculated that the peninsula could be transformed into a Soviet ‘‘irrigated Eden”51° and
attain food self-sufficiency within twenty years. True enough, for the time being, the
man-made river from the nuclear desalination plant would need to be complemented
by freshwater pipelines from the Emba and Amu-Dar’ia rivers,52 but in the long term, the
diversion of Siberian rivers - again to be achieved by courtesy of the peaceful atoms dig-
ging canals and pumping water uphill - would take care of the problem.53 In a next step,
the “Mangyshlak development precedent” was to be broadly applied to other regions of
the country and potentially worldwide.54 Confident of having established the viability of
large-scale breeder and desalination technologies,55 the atomshchiki proposed the con-
struction of a number of gargantuan nuclear water desalination plants along the USSR’s
arid southern flank56 from Southern Ukraine to Vladivostok.57 Third World countries
were also targeted as potential beneficiaries and customers of the technology.58 The “first
city of the Scientific-Technological Revolution” thus not only expressed a prescriptive
technopolitical regime - the experience gathered here also helped to further define it,
and stretched the horizon of what seemed possible.

On the downside, the creation of new nature came with a hefty ecological price tag.
Firstly, the extraction of Mangyshlak’s natural riches consumed and permanently de-
graded enormous quantities of other valuable resources - especially freshwater, which
was lavishly used in both uranium mining and oil production. As a result, the freshwater
consumption of Shevchenko’s industrial complex was far greater than what the nuclear
desalination plant could provide, and in the early 1980s, a freshwater pipeline from the
Volga estuary to Mangyshlak was built to meet the demand.59 Second, the extraction
of Mangyshlak’s resources released large amounts of toxic and radioactive pollutants
into the environment, which were disposed of in a rather careless manner. For years,
the uranium processing factory dumped its tailings unceremoniously into the nearest
depression, where they formed a constantly expanding pond that was soon surrounded
by the sprawling city’s cynically named “lakeside” residential district - a practice that
was irresponsible even by the lax Soviet standards of the day. In 1965, the uranium pro-
cessing plant and its tailings reservoir were transferred to the industrial area outside the
city limits,60 but with both the city and the reservoir growing at a quick pace, the cloaca
soon came dangerously close to the city again.61 After some 30 years of operation, a lake
of 72 square kilometres filled the Koshkar-ata depression a mere 5 kilometres North of
the city brimming with more than 350 million tons of radioactive waste, acids and heavy
metals.62 Finally, the extensive extraction of resources from the depths of the peninsula
and the deposition of waste on its surface (most notably in the tailings reservoir) affect-
ed not only the ecology, but also the geology of the peninsula in ways so profound that
they were eventually believed to prompt seismic activities (see below).63

While Shevchenkos industries were thus detrimental to the environment even in
times of normal operation, the Chernobyl disaster of 1986 threw into sharp profile the
inherent risk of large-scale technogenic accidents that loomed over Shevchenkos nucle-
ar complex. Thousands of specialists who were dispatched from Shevchenko to Ukraine
to participate in disaster mitigation experienced the catastrophe on their own bodies,64
and while Shevchenkos authorities hurried to assure the local public that the BN-350
reactor was a fundamentally different and inherently safe design, those in the know were
well aware that it, too, had experienced several near-catastrophes over the course of its
operation. Shortly after start-up, leaks in the steam generators had provoked repeated
sodium-water fires which had threatened to disrupt the reactor s cooling system. Fuel
rod claddings embrittled prematurely under the conditions of intense irradiation, and
radioactive decay within the rods released gases which caused the rods to swell - a phe-
nomenon which had not been fully understood when the reactor was designed, and
which, in the initial phase of operation, caused fuel rods to leak and become jammed
in the reactor core.65 Several years into operation, used fuel elements became stuck in
a transportation mechanism without sufficient cooling, and a major accident was only
narrowly avoided.66 In 1984, the reactor s power supply failed, and with the only back-
up system out of order, it was pure luck that the electricity supply could be restored
in time.67 At the PGMK, uranium processing posed its own set of security problems,
including spills of radioactive pulp and sporadic releases of toxic gases.68 Still, these dan-
gers paled in comparison to the risks associated with the combine s large-scale produc-
tion of chemical agents and fertilisers, which involved handling large quantities of high-
ly explosive substances.69 The same held true for local petrochemistry, and in particular
the citys plastics plant.70

Shevchenkos large-scale technologies hence entailed a significant number of risks
before the possible natural causes of technological accidents were even considered. No
wonder, then, that the potential for envirotechnical disasters - disasters resulting from
an interaction of natural and technological factors - was initially ignored.71 Eventually,
however, Soviet technologists in Mangyshlak found themselves confronted with the in-
sight that nature was prone to interfering with their technological designs and did not
behave as assumed in their oversimplified calculations. The suspicion that they might
be in the process of improving nature for the worse struck them for the first time when
the level of the Caspian Sea, which had kept falling for most of the 20th century, started
to rise quickly in the late 1970s. By the 1990s, it was up by two and a half meters from its
historic low and still continued to rise unabatedly.72 This development revealed not only
the foolishness of the Siberian river diversion project, which had been intended, among
other things, to provide additional influx for the Caspian Sea. It also highlighted the
carelessness that had guided Soviet planners when siting expensive technological arte-
facts in close proximity to the shoreline. In 1991, a government commission found that
any further rise in sea levels would render Mangyshlak’s regional economy, including
its harbour, railway communications, many oil wells and, above all, the Atomic Energy
Combine, largely non-functional, and ordered the immediate construction of protec-
tive dams.73

On December 16th, 1987, a gigantic landslide in the open-pit uranium mine alerted
the authorities in Mangyshlak to yet another risk they had hitherto ignored. In the ab-
sence of other plausible causes, research into the seismic activity of the peninsula was
commissioned - and produced worrying results. Satellite photographs and the study of
old mosque chronicles provided evidence of vigorous earthquakes in close proximity
to the city of Shevchenko in historic and prehistoric times. For the present, researchers
observed a notable intensification of tectonic processes in the region and advised the
city authorities to brace for a major earthquake.74 This was alarming news in view of
the fact that Mangyshlak had hitherto counted among the seismically inactive regions
of the country, as a consequence of which even its nuclear power plant lacked any kind
of earthquake protection, and retrofitting it was found to be prohibitively expensive.75 76
And there was yet another troubling observation - namely that the human transfor-
mation of nature, above all the extraction of oil, gas and ground water from the penin-
sulas depths and the increasing pressure exerted on its surface by the growing tailings
reservoir Koshkar-ata, was at least partially to blame for the imminent danger.74 Against
this background and attuned to the apocalyptic mood of the period, Shevchenkos once
overly confident technologists developed gloomy doomsday scenarios for a combined
natural and man-made disaster: a strong earthquake would trigger immense explosions
at Shevchenkos chemical industries and at the thermal power station located in the im-
mediate vicinity of the nuclear power plant. Shock-waves from the explosions, com-
bined with initial earthquake damage, would then wreak havoc upon the BN-350, with
catastrophic consequences for the city.77

Awareness of ecological problems peaked in the very last years of the Soviet peri-
od, when an ambitious plan for the ecological recovery of the region was elaborated.
However, calls for assistance to Moscow and Almaty went largely unanswered, and af-
ter the collapse of the Soviet Union, pressing socio-economic problems eclipsed the
regions ecological calamities, leaving the latter largely unsolved.78 Conservation of the
Koshkar-ata tailings lake in particular proved beyond the means of local and national
authorities, finally prompting Kazakhstan’s nuclear corporation Kazatomprom to come
up with an unconventional solution to the problem in 2001. Converting Aqtau’s for-
mer uranium mine into a permanent repository for nuclear waste and selling storage
capacities to foreign customers might go a long way towards financing the clean-up of
radiation legacies not only in Mangyshlak, but throughout the country, it suggested.79
Resistance from the national parliament stopped the project in its tracks and as a result,
Aqtau escaped the fate of becoming the nuclear dumping ground for the entire world,
but the Koshkar-ata problem also remained unsolved.80 In recent years, the persisting
radiation legacy has not discouraged local authorities from planning an ambitious city
expansion project in close proximity to the Koshkar-Ata, or from promoting the region
as a destination for eco-tourism.

Sotsgorod, Prison Camp and Shantytown: the Social Dimension

Recent studies have analysed the social implications of nuclear technopolitics in a varie-
ty of historical and geographical settings, describing how national nuclear programmes
produced powerful techno-scientific elites and privileged workforces, but how they also
brought forth deprived social groups which suffered disproportionately from the eco-
logical and health hazards of the industry and often served as cheap reservoirs of dis-
posable labourers.81 In many cases, the principles of segregation structuring the nuclear
sector have been found to align with pre-existing societal fault lines, sometimes further
reinforcing them. Thus, studies of the early Soviet nuclear programme have pointed out
its heavy reliance on penal labour/1 and more recently Hecht has analysed the global
division of labour and risk allocation of the atomic age in postcolonial terms, pointing
out that many of its most hazardous test sites and production facilities were located in
formerly colonial or quasi-colonial spaces, among which she also counts Soviet Kazakh-
stan.82 83

A case study of Shevchenko sheds light on many of these aspects while complement-
ing existing research with a focus on the post-Stalinist period and the non-Slavic part of
the Soviet Union. In a confined geographical space, the nuclear complex of Mangyshlak
combined a model city with a sprawling network of forced labour camps and a subur-
ban shantytown that was predominantly Kazakh in terms of its ethnic composition. The
coexistence of these tightly delineated, but closely interlocking social microcosms high-
lights a puzzling synchrony of late Soviet attempts at technocratic governmentality with
mobilisation and segregation practices carried over from Stalinist industrialisation - a
synchrony which many coeval observers perceived as deeply anachronistic, but which
nevertheless remained, I will argue, systemic of late Soviet authoritarian modernisation.

According to the original script of de-Stalinisation, forced labour was to be phased
out before large-scale construction even started in Mangyshlak. In 1956, the Council of
Ministers instructed Sredmash to make the transition to Tree wage labour” - something
that was easier said than done at a time when the construction sites of the ministry
still employed close to 100,000 convicts throughout the USSR.84 Swapping prisoners for
vol'nonaemniki (free wage labourers) required both a fundamental revision of the min-
istry’s recruitment practices and a thorough-going transformation of what had hitherto
essentially been an archipelago of prison camps into a network of full-fledged cities.85 86
Shevchenko was one of the first atomic cities to showcase this transformation. When it
was accorded a State Prize for Architecture in 1977, the jury argued that it not only em-
bodied the best features of Soviet urban planning, such as clearly separated housing and
industrial districts, but also set itself apart from other Soviet new towns such as Tol’iatti
in that it was built to human scale and thoughtfully embedded into the surrounding
landscape, making it “a very humane” and “truly socialist city”.84

In their efforts to populate the new town and staff its industries with the required
specialists, the authorities combined ministerial dispatch mechanisms with extensive
labour recruitment campaigns. In urgent need of hands, employers resorted to labour
piracy among the workforce of nuclear companion enterprises87 and routinely ignored
the citys strict passport regime, thereby opening the doors not only to highly motivated
specialists, but also to “gamblers and adventurers”, as the city’s security organs com-
plained. In 1971, more than 10,000 people joined a city population that was approach-
ing 70,000, with more than 3000 leaving within the same timespan88 - ratios that made
the city resemble the quicksand societies of Stalinist-era construction sites. To make
sought-after specialists stay, comprehensive incentives were offered, including wage bo-
nuses and preferential allocation of living spaces and consumer goods in addition to
attractive career opportunities. The highly dynamic bargaining processes that ensued
went far beyond what is usually framed as a late Soviet little deal between the govern-
ment and the governed.89 Moving to the remote desert city was undoubtedly a big deal
for those who left their former lives behind in exchange for a chance to participate in
shaping the country’s future, or, more prosaically, in pursuit of material benefits. Nor
were the multi-billion investments in the region’s development marginal from the So-
viet authorities’ perspective.90 Rather, they epitomised a massive effort on the part of the
Khrushchev and Brezhnev administrations to rekindle the socio-economic dynamics
of the country by technopolitical means. In this scheme, new science and technology
towns served the dual purpose of techno-scientific hotspots intended to keep the prolif-
erating technocratic elites of the country engaged in the Soviet project and of miniature
utopias designed to pre-empt the blessings of the Scientific-Technological Revolution
for society at large.91 Shevchenko performed well on both counts, providing thousands
of white-collar workers with employment at the cutting edge of Soviet technological
development while also realising the vision of an integrative and equitable post-Stalin-
ist society, albeit limited to a privileged urban microcosm. Boasting a well-developed
social sphere, it escaped the problematic bachelor societies of other Soviet pioneering
towns and quickly became a city of nuclear families92 93 where socially and ethnically mixed
neighbourhoods created an impression of equality, and where skilled workers joined
reactor engineers in enjoying an advanced Soviet way of life92

Yet the relative internal homogeneity of the urban social microcosm could not dis-
tract from the fact that contrary to the initial grand design, the model city of Shevchen-
ko, like its Stalin-era predecessors, remained heavily dependent on forced labour. Given
the remote and uninviting location of the site, recruitment campaigns on the regular So-
viet labour market missed their targets by a wide margin, nor did seemingly mild forms
of unfree labour, such as the employment of construction soldiers/03 probationers and
recently released convicts suffice to eliminate the labour shortage.94 95 In these circum-
stances, widespread dissatisfaction with the consequences of the recent post-Stalinist
reforms of the penitentiary system played into the hands of those who continued to
consider forced labour a valid option. The extreme desert conditions in Mangyshlak
certainly promised to go a long way towards satisfying the popular contemporary de-
mand that “the camp should not be a holiday resort”.96 In i960, Ruben A. Grigorian,
head of the Mangyshlak construction site, received notice from Moscow that the work-
force requirements of his site would henceforth be met mainly with convicts, and that
their numbers would steadily increase97 - as in fact they did, reaching a level of 12,000-
20,000 men by the early 1970s.98 In their overwhelming majority, the convicts were de-
ployed for urban and industrial construction, including highly sensitive complexes such
as the nuclear power plant.99 Later, they were also dispatched to the construction sites
of the oil and gas industry,100 and when demand from the building industries eventually
decreased, the Ministry of the Interior employed the convicts in Mangyshlak in the pro-
duction of agricultural machinery.101

The resulting situation was deeply unsatisfactory in social, economic and ideologi-
cal terms. Within the camps, felons terrorised their less experienced fellow convicts
and raised mutinies (an incident in 1972 left 16 dead and 11 injured),102 at the construc-
tion sites, they threatened free wage labourers.103 Refusal to work, theft and sabotage
jeopardised even the most important construction projects, causing the authorities to
question the rationality of convict labour repeatedly/104 and ex-prisoners who stayed in
the city caused local crime rates to soar.105 Worst of all, the highly visible presence of
forced labour throughout the city served as a constant reminder of the Stalinist herit-
age of coercion that defied the political sermons about the enthusiasm of Communist
construction.106 The ideological conundrum was rendered all the more acute by the fact
that in contrast to camps-turned-cities like Vorkuta or Magadan, Shevchenkos camp
complex was not a hangover from the GULag, but was established on a clean slate well
after Stalins death.107 Granted, the Soviet detention system did not revert to its Stalin-
ist state even after the counter-reforms of the early 1960s; to reign in arbitrariness and
despotism, the detainment and the employment of convicts were administratively dis-
entangled108 and regular inspections were introduced.109 The fact that slowdowns and
outright refusal to work became mass phenomena in the 1970s110 certainly suggests that
camp authorities enforced discipline less draconically than in Stalinist times. To what
extent Mangyshlak’s camp complex was representative of larger Soviet realities cannot
be reliably determined in the absence of further case studies, but anecdotal evidence
and declassified CIA reports suggest that convict labour continued to be a widespread
phenomenon on large-scale construction sites throughout the post-Stalinist Soviet Un-
ion.111 The example of Shevchenko certainly makes a strong case for the assumption that
it remained systemic of a modernising regime whose continued preference for large-
scale, short-timeframe projects in remote regions of the country by and large perpetu-
ated the patterns of Stalinist industrialisation.112 Inevitably, the economic rationality of
these projects quickly clashed with the intention to rely solely on free wage labour, and
when the conflict became obvious, the regime resolved it in favour of its economic pri-
orities.

Unlike the situation of the prison camp inmates, the underprivileged status of the
titular nationality resulted from oversight and neglect on the part of the authorities
rather than from deliberate arrangements. Post-Stalinist propaganda portrayed Man-
gyshlak as a tabula rasa where representatives of all Soviet nationalities joined forces in
a heroic effort to conquer the empty desert - a narrative that was purposefully oblivious
of the regions Stalinist prehistory that had seen a brutal sedentarization and collectiv-
isation drive which had killed or put to flight all but 26,000 out of the regions original
350,000 seasonal nomadic inhabitants in the early 1930s.113 When the industrial develop-
ment of the region triggered a momentous demographic influx from the Slavic heartland
and the Northern Caucasus region of the USSR three decades later, the involvement of
ethnic Kazakhs in the nuclear industries remained marginal;114 in the oil and gas sector,
they often found themselves in disadvantaged positions vis-a-vis the generally better
trained and paid specialists from Azerbaijan and Chechnya. Although the situation
caused sporadic outbursts of frustration, it seemed to go largely unnoticed until Decem-
ber 1986, when nationalist upheavals in Alma-Ata compelled the authorities to critically
reappraise their previous nationality policy. In Mangyshlak; investigations revealed an
appalling economic inequity between the highly qualified immigrants that formed the
core of the regions industrial workforce and an underprivileged Kazakh population lit-
erally living on the fringes of society often in slum-like suburbs of the regions modern
atomic and oil production cities.115 The glaring lack of affirmative action vis-a-vis the titu-
lar nationality which lay at the basis of this situation owed much to the fact that Kazakh
republican authorities had but a marginal say in the development of the region/116 which
instead was largely determined by nationally agnostic, all-Union industrial ministries
that preferred to import their workforces rather than train them locally. Again, this was a
typical and therefore systemic trait of the large-scale development projects that the late
Soviet modernising regime continued to favour.117 Under these circumstances, token
attempts to improve interethnic relations118 could not prevent a local eruption of Kazakh
nationalist unrest in 1989 that set in motion a mass exodus of the non-titular popula-
tion119 - a process that only intensified under the influence of the regions post-Soviet
economic collapse in the 1990s.

Nuclear Techno-Diplomacy: the Transnational Dimension

Throughout the Cold War, nuclear power in both its military and civilian varieties was
not only a matter of supreme national importance and international prestige for the
countries that engaged in it.120 From the very beginning, it was also understood as an
inherently transnational endeavour in terms of its techno-scientific preconditions and
socio-economic consequences/121 and as such it quickly became the subject of bilater-
al, multilateral and supranational techno-diplomacy.122 But while the "centrality of the
nuclear” is generally undisputed in Cold War historiography/123 nuclear techno-scien-
tific encounters across the block divide have not received due attention.124 The case of
Shevchenko highlights that there is much to discover in this field. Between 1965 and
1991, more than sixty delegations from capitalist, socialist and developing countries vis-
ited the Mangyshlak Atomic Energy Combine (MAEK)134 and in a single, fairly typical
year (1977), a dozen of its representatives spent anywhere from one to twelve weeks at
nuclear facilities in France, the United States, Great Britain, West and East Germany and
the CSSR under the terms of bilateral collaboration agreements.135

Soviet scientists introduced Shevchenkos BN-350 to an international audience at
the Third Geneva Conference in 1964, at a time when breeder reactors elicited both pro-
found fears and high hopes.136 More than any other nuclear technology, their plutonium-
breeding capacity not only promised to revolutionise civilian power generation, but also
threatened to further accelerate the nuclear arms race which had only just brought the
world to the brink of annihilation during the Cuban missile crisis. In response to these
concerns, political decision-makers on both sides of the bloc divide reached a tacit
agreement on the necessity to keep a watchful eye on each other s ambitions in the field.
Also, since breeders were seen as untested high-risk technology at the time, an inter-
national exchange of experience seemed crucial to avert accidents.137 An international
conference on breeders, convened in Detroit in 1965 by the American Nuclear Society
and attended by a high-calibre Soviet delegation which disclosed substantial informa-
tion about the BN-350,138 helped jump-start the circulation of relevant knowledge in the
global scientific community.139

In the aftermath of this conference, contacts developed most rapidly with de Gaulle s
France, which had only just joined the club of nuclear weapons states and pursued a poli-
cy of rapprochement towards the USSR in an attempt to emancipate itself from NATO.
In 1966 and 1967, the two countries concluded comprehensive cooperation agreements
that focused on nuclear desalination and breeder technology - key areas in both sides’
nuclear strategies at the time.140 In particular, the USSR and France were developing the
first two large-scale breeders in the world in parallel to each other: Shevchenkos BN-350
and the French Phenix at Marcoule.141 Given the fact that Soviet breeder research pre-
dated the French efforts by several years, the French had a keen interest in an exchange
of knowledge and experience,142 and Shevchenkos MAEK soon became a regular desti-
nation for sizeable delegations of French breeder specialists, usually headed by Fernand
Conte, the designated director of the French Phenix. The staff of the BN-350 received in-

Contacts with the United States in the field of the utilisation of atomic energy for
peaceful purposes were established early on in the context of the Geneva conferences,
and formalised by a first bilateral memorandum in 1959 which paved the way for
high-calibre mutual visits in the early 1960s.129 The Soviet leadership valued the pros-
pect of cooperation highly enough to invite U. S. nuclear scientist Glenn Seaborg to an
audience in the Kremlin in 1963, during which Leonid Brezhnev, then chairman of the
Presidium of the Supreme Soviet, asked him for an honest appraisal of the work of his
Soviet colleagues. Seaborg replied that American and Soviet scientists were working in
very similar directions, and agreed with Brezhnev that closer cooperation would be in
the best interests of both sides.130 Taking place just one year after the Cuban missile cri-
sis, this meeting marked a first tentative attempt to ease geopolitical tensions by finding
common ground in the realm of a shared technocratic rationality.

A year after this conversation, a high-profile delegation of American nuclear special-
ists, including Seaborg, visited Shevchenko for the first time and was offered a glimpse
of the BN-350 under construction.131 At a time when the American Enrico Fermi I ex-
perimental breeder was nearing completion, this was a somewhat ambiguous move of
Soviet techno-diplomacy that could be seen either as a trust-building measure or as a
demonstration that the Soviet Union was willing to compete with the U. S. in this cru-
cial field of nuclear dual-use technology. US-Soviet nuclear techno-diplomacy received
a major boost when Richard Nixon s visit to Moscow in 1972 inaugurated a new phase of
detente. Again, geopolitics and technopolitics went hand in hand, as became clear dur-
ing Brezhnev s return visit to the U. S. one year later, when both sides not only pledged
to refrain from a nuclear first strike against each other, but also agreed to deepen their
civilian nuclear cooperation within the framework of a newly founded US-USSR Joint
Committee on Cooperation in the Peaceful Uses of Atomic Energy.132 Nixon s ardent advoca-
cy of nuclear energy and his determination to commission the United States’ first large-
scale breeder reactor - similar in size and technology to the BN-350 - at Clinch River
by 1980 lent significant momentum to these plans.133 Given that the previous U. S. foray
into breeder reactors had ended in a partial core meltdown of the Fermi I in 1966, the
American side was profoundly interested in Soviet operational experience with large-
scale breeder reactors, which made Shevchenko a top destination for U. S. delegations.134

A closer look at a particular U. S. visit to Shevchenko in December 1976 highlights
the specific blend of openness and secrecy that characterised these contacts.135 Upon ar-
rival, the American delegation, headed by Robert Seamans, a high-ranking official of the
American atomic power administration with previous experience in the top echelons of
the U. S. military missile programme, was met by the Soviet deputy minister for atomic
energy, Andranik Petrosiants, and taken on a tour of the reactor facility along a pains-
takingly predetermined itinerary that led the American guests to the control room, the
reactor hall and the desalination batteries.136 To uphold the fiction of the reactor s pure-
ly civilian character, its military security staff had been disguised in civilian clothing.137
During the ensuing meeting, a thoroughly compiled collection of declassified papers
regulated the technical details that the Soviet hosts were allowed to disclose to their
American guests, and comprehensive volumes of questions and answers helped them
remember what facts, half-truths and disinformation they had given their American col-
leagues on earlier occasions, so that they did not get lost in contradictions.138 Despite
such precautions, the matters discussed were substantial. In particular, the American
guests proposed installing a prototype steam generator of American design and manu-
facture in the BN-350 - one of the most problematic pieces of equipment of fast reac-
tors. “As this steam generator would be of the type that the U. S. intends to use in its
Clinch River Fast-Breeder Reactor Facility” an American diplomatic report explained,
“the early installation of a generator in the BN-350 would provide the U. S. valuable
experience on its own equipment.”139 The U. S. side thus hoped to test-run the trou-
blesome component in a Soviet reactor where a possible accident would go unnoticed
by the American public, while Soviet nuclear engineers were interested in studying the
American approach to one of the most notorious pitfalls of breeder reactor design that
also continued to plague the BN-350 s operation. Considerable effort was put into the
project thereafter until it was stopped in its tracks for reasons relating to U. S. domestic
policy. In January 1977, Jimmy Carter assumed office as president and immediately or-
dered a halt to the controversial U. S. breeder programme.140 Subsequently, the focus of
U. S.-Soviet cooperation shifted to more theoretical fields such as high energy physics.

Summing up, it becomes clear that nuclear technoscience was framed differently in
the arena of transnational cooperation than it was on the stage of international show-
casing. Rather than claiming a specifically Soviet imaginary of atomic-powered Com-
munism, when dealing professionally with their Western peers the Soviet atomshchiki
treated nuclear physics and engineering as “portable knowledge”141 that was valid irre-
spective of the political context in which it was produced or applied. On one occasion,
the journal Soviet Atomic Energy explicitly praised Soviet-French nuclear collaboration
as “an excellent example of mutually advantageous collaboration between governments
with a different social order”.142 Undoubtedly, drawing clear distinctions between tech-
nology and ideology, technopolitics and geopolitics helped establish techno-diplomacy
as an alternative channel of superpower relations where East and West interacted not so
much as potential opponents in a nuclear war, but rather as shareholders of a common
technocratic rationality.143

Back to the Future? Outlook and Conclusions

Against the backdrop of a barren shoreline framed by endless sand dunes, two large
vessels, painted in immaculate white, dock at a concrete pier. A powerful blue spark of
energy emerges from one of them and identifies the ship as a floating reactor, accom-
panied by a nuclear-powered desalination vessel. In tandem, they set to work immedi-
ately. Sending electricity and freshwater to the shore, they let palm trees and skyscrap-
ers sprout from the parched ground like a mirage. To date, this desert-turned-garden
redux lives only on the computer screens of Rosatoms marketing department, but a
prototype floating reactor is currently approaching completion, and in 2015 the state
corporation which is Sredmash’s present-day successor started accepting internation-
al orders for nuclear power plants integrated with large capacity desalination facilities.
Forecasting a global freshwater deficit of up to 2 trillion cubic metres by 2025, Rosatom
sees atomic-powered water desalination as a highly promising business perspective.144
Breeder reactors, too, are back on Rosatom’s agenda. Recently, the BN-350S technologi-
cal grandson, the BN-800, has come online at Beloiarsk as part of an ambitious innova-
tion programme dubbed "breakthrough” (proryv), making Russia one of the few coun-
tries to still pursue development of the controversial reactor type.145 The technologies
that the Soviet Union pioneered at Shevchenko are thus again at the core of Rosatom’s
domestic and international strategies today,146 147 and the “Mangyshlak precedent” features
prominently as proof of concept in these visions.145

As for Aqtau (as Shevchenko is called today), computer animations every bit as pol-
ished as Rosatom’s depict a hyper-modern city expansion project for up to one million
inhabitants, complete with skyscrapers, shopping malls and marinas.148 Purposefully
reminiscent of the Arabian Emirates on the Persian Gulf, the new Aqtau City is primar-
ily designed as a hub for the development of the promising offshore oil fields of Kasha-
gan,149 but Kazakhstan’s recent advancement to global leadership in uranium production
has also rekindled the country’s interest in nuclear power. Given Aqtau’s prior integra-
tion into the Soviet nuclear project, a new reactor for the city, to be built in coopera-
tion with Rosatom, seems like the most promising perspective for Kazakhstan to regain
access to the technology. In return, MAEK’s specialists have already joined Rosatom’s
expert council on water desalination to share their knowledge and experience in the
field.150 Since the groundbreaking ceremony for Aqtau City in 2008, local economic
problems and the international financial crisis have stalled both the city’s expansion and
the new reactor project, but in view of the region’s wealth in natural resources, it may still
materialise in the future.

In conclusion, then, what insights into post-Stalinist Soviet history and its aftermath
are to be gained from studying the manifestations of nuclear technopolitics in a remote,
parched desert peninsula? To begin with, the recognition that the second phase of the
Soviet nuclear project was characterised by profound ambiguities, and that these ambi-
guities were increasingly acknowledged by the historical actors themselves. The deeper
atomic-powered Communism was woven into the socio-political and envirotechnical
fabric of the Soviet Union, the more clear-cut dichotomies gave way to perplexing am-
biguities. Shevchenkos atom was both a worker and a soldier, it improved and harmed
nature, and it brought about a social utopia alongside its dystopian counterpart. Inevita-
bly, the co-production of ideas, materiality, and sociality yielded unexpected and often
undesired results.

When the Chernobyl reactor exploded in 1986, many believed that atomic-powered
Communism would be buried once and for all under the sarcophagus of reactor four.151
Five years later, the Communist project was indeed defunct, but another quarter of a
century later, nuclear visions are gaining new momentum in the post-Soviet space, par-
ticularly in Russia and Kazakhstan. The foundations for this renaissance were laid in the
aftermath of Chernobyl, when representatives of nuclear technoscience engaged in a
purification discourse which dissociated atomic power from “Communism”, casting the
Soviet political and economic system as the main restraining factor that had hitherto sti-
fled and corrupted their field. Matched by a rational technocratic regime, purified nucle-
ar technoscience would truly come into its own, they suggested.152 In the new millenni-
um, the Putin regime recommended itself as just such a partner, once again embracing,
ironically in much the same way as its Soviet predecessor, the promise of high-modern
progress that nuclear physics and engineering seemed to hold.

The ensuing renovation of nuclear technoscience masked rather than severed the
industry’s comprehensive ties to the Soviet past - in fact, the underlying continuities
of actors, practices and artefacts were essential for its relaunch. In more fundamental
terms, it can be argued that post-Soviet nuclear technopolitics perpetuate the Soviet-era
functional alliance between big science and technology and what has been generalised
as authoritarian modernisation that formed in the Soviet era.153 It has been observed that
the large-scale, top-down modernisation programmes that authoritarian regimes tend
to implement in response to military or economic challenges typically result in techno-
political regimes that are heavily invested in the chosen trajectory and therefore averse
to change.154 The involvement of big science and technology further amplifies the in-
herent inertia of incumbent sociotechnical regimes by the sheer scale and scope of the
enduring artefacts, materialities, practices and status attributions which they produce.
Clearly, nuclear reactors, atomic bombs and radioactive waste are not easily disposed of
and require sustained maintenance irrespective of whether they are decommissioned or
further utilised.155 In view of what is at stake - sunk investments in machines, infrastruc-
tures and skills, adaptation of lifestyles to technical systems156 - the prospect of “fixing”
rather than abandoning the products of nuclear technoscience is often perceived and
presented as the less costly option - a line of thought which occasionally culminates
in the somewhat paradoxical argument that Russia and Kazakhstan have already paid
too high a price in financial, ecological and social terms for their involvement in nuclear
modernity to now dismiss it without cashing in on prior investments.

A historical perspective can thus serve to remind us that the post-Soviet relaunch
of nuclear technopolitics reproduces, for both parties involved, not only the perceived
opportunities, but also the inherent risks of its Soviet model. In the event of success,
the two partners are supposed to lend authority and legitimacy to each other, but they
also risk discrediting and delegitimising each other in the event of continued incompe-
tence or outright failure. Rosatoms international reputation is already undermined by
its status as a state corporation that may serve a geopolitical agenda, and, vice versa, it
seems highly unlikely that the Putin administration could survive another major nuclear
disaster.157

Apart from large-scale nuclear accidents, mega-projects probably highlight the close
link between the fates of techno-scientific and political regimes more clearly than any-
thing else. As typical “delivery vehicles” of authoritarian modernisation, they afford
these regimes an opportunity to showcase their transformative clout, but at the same
time they carry a significant risk of failure. And even if successful, they communicate
an ambiguous message, privileging, as they do, “spatial polarisation over spatial equali-
sation” and systemic reform.158 Actualising such miniature utopias159 hence inevitably
turns them into heterotopias of their surrounding realities160 - places above and ahead of
their context which simultaneously epitomise what the regime is all about and what it
persistently fails to achieve as a whole. This will apply to Aqtau City, if it ever becomes a
reality, in much the same way as it held true for Shevchenko.
